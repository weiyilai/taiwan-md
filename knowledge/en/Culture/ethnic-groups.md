# Taiwan's Ethnic Groups

> **30-Second Overview:** In 2016, Taiwan's president stood inside the Presidential Office and delivered a formal apology to Indigenous peoples for four hundred years of harm — while a group of tribal members was held back by police shields outside, refusing to accept the apology at all. Taiwan recognizes five major ethnic groups (Minnan, Hakka, Indigenous, Mainlanders, and New Immigrants), but this classification was only invented in 1993, and it left certain people out from the start.

On August 1, 2016, just before dawn in Taipei, a millet stalk was set alight at the main entrance of the Presidential Office.

Smoke drifted upward. This is Indigenous ritual — smoke summons the ancestors to witness. Bunun elder Hu Jin-niang poured rice wine at the Chingguo Hall so the spirits could "meet" the newly inaugurated Tsai Ing-wen. Then Tsai read aloud a document unprecedented in Taiwan's history:

> **✦** "On behalf of the government, I want to express our deepest apologies to all Indigenous peoples. For the suffering and unjust treatment you have endured over the past four hundred years, I apologize on behalf of the government." — Tsai Ing-wen, Indigenous Peoples' Day, 2016

Outside the front gate, another group of Indigenous people was being kept back by police shields. They called those who had walked inside "collaborators," and accused Tsai of bringing elders in behind closed doors to accept an apology on their behalf.

That image — the burning millet stalk, the apology document, and the shields outside — is almost a perfect diagram of Taiwan's ethnic politics: historical wounds, governmental gestures, and deep disagreement among affected communities about whether any of it is enough.

## How "Four Major Ethnic Groups" Was Invented

The "four major ethnic groups" framework that Taiwanese people now take as natural was created in 1993.

DPP legislators Lin Zhuo-shui (林濁水) and Ye Ju-lan (葉菊蘭) formally proposed the classification: Indigenous peoples, Minnan (Hoklo), Hakka, and Mainlanders. It gave Taiwan a shared vocabulary for discussing ethnic identity for the first time. But this wasn't a classification that emerged organically from history — it was a political construction, a tool of the localization movement that packaged "Taiwanese people" into four legible groups to counter what activists saw as Mainlander cultural dominance.

Scholar Wang Fu-chang, in _Ethnic Imagination in Contemporary Taiwan Society_, points out the framework's internal contradictions: it places fundamentally different kinds of distinctions on the same conceptual plane. The line between Indigenous and Han peoples is not the same kind of line as the one between Minnan and Hakka. Treating them as equivalent is useful politically but misleading analytically.

And from the beginning, certain people were left out of the count.

> **📝 Editor's Note**
> The "four major ethnic groups" framework gave Taiwan a common self-description — but it also hardened a strange logic: Indigenous peoples count as a single "ethnic group," yet Minnan people are actually a cluster of three distinct accent communities. "Mainlanders" includes people from Guangdong, Shandong, Inner Mongolia, and Tibet all under one label. The framework works well enough for political conversation. As a description of social reality, it was imprecise from day one.

## Where Are the Plains Indigenous Peoples?

In 1956, the ROC government conducted Taiwan's first modern census — and simply deleted the "Plains Indigenous" category from the form.

The Plains Indigenous peoples (平埔族) were Taiwan's first group to encounter outside settlers — the Ketagalan, Siraya, Makatao, and others. They were the first to be assimilated under Qing rule, and that early assimilation cost them their legal recognition. When the "four major ethnic groups" framework was established, "Indigenous peoples" referred to the 16 officially recognized groups. Plains Indigenous descendants fell outside the category, and their status remains legally unresolved today.

In 2001, 400 Plains Indigenous representatives entered the Legislative Yuan to demand restoration of Indigenous status. In 2010, representatives filed a complaint to the United Nations against the Council of Indigenous Peoples for refusing to recognize them. In 2016, Tsai Ing-wen's apology included a promise to resolve the issue. Legislation has stalled ever since.

When people cite Taiwan's "four major ethnic groups," some people are already missing from that count.

## The Name That Took Ten Years

Before the "four major ethnic groups" framework existed, even the term "Indigenous peoples" was itself the product of a decade of political struggle.

Before 1994, Taiwan's official terminology for the island's original inhabitants was **shān bāo** (山胞) — "mountain compatriots." Earlier designations included "番" (barbarians), "高砂族" (Takasago peoples, a Japanese colonial term), and "山地山胞." In 1984, the Taiwan Indigenous Peoples Rights Promotion Association was founded and launched the Name Rectification Movement. Exactly ten years of street protests and constitutional lobbying later, **on August 1, 1994**, "shān bāo" disappeared from the ROC Constitution and was replaced with "yuánzhùmín" (原住民, Indigenous peoples).

That date became Indigenous Peoples' Day — which is why Tsai Ing-wen chose it for her apology exactly 22 years later.

Taiwan currently officially recognizes **16 Indigenous nations**, including the Amis, Atayal, Paiwan, Bunun, Tao, and others, with a combined population of approximately **614,000** (February 2025, Council of Indigenous Peoples), representing 2.6% of Taiwan's total population. Land rights in traditional territories remain contested: the Indigenous Peoples Basic Act Article 21 requires informed tribal consent before development proceeds in traditional domains, but whether "traditional domain" includes privately owned land within those areas has been interpreted differently by the Executive Yuan and the Supreme Administrative Court — and Amis communities on the east coast have been fighting development projects in those gray zones for years.

> **⚠️ Disputed Territory**
> Multiple Indigenous organizations criticized the 2016 apology as unfulfilled. Nuclear waste remains stored on Tao (Yami) land on Orchid Island. The traditional territory demarcation regulations have been criticized for effectively stripping Indigenous peoples of meaningful veto power over large-scale development on privately held land within their ancestral domains. As activist Mayaw Biho noted, the regulations can transform the "informed consent" requirement into a formality. The apology produced a document. Its implementation is a different story.

## 1988: Sun Yat-sen in a Face Mask

Rewind to December 28, 1988. Taipei streets.

At the front of a protest march, someone carried a framed portrait of Sun Yat-sen — with a surgical mask over his face. Provincial Assembly member Fu Wen-zheng led the crowd, reading the memorial address in Hakka: "We, the Hakka descendants, stand before your spirit and ask you to bless the Hakka people to unite, to ensure that the Hakka language survives forever, and that the Hakka people remain hardworking and capable."

The protest banners read: "If the founding father Sun Yat-sen were still alive, he couldn't speak his own Hakka language on television."

This was the **Return Our Mother Tongue Movement** — the Hakka community's first large-scale street protest. More than **10,000 people** marched to oppose the Broadcasting and Television Act, which prohibited television stations from airing programs in "dialects." In that era, speaking Hakka on TV was banned — exactly as speaking Taiwanese was banned.

Did the march change anything? Yes — but slowly. Dialect broadcasting was gradually liberalized starting in 1991. In 2003, Hakka TV launched. In 2019, the National Language Development Act designated Taiwanese, Hakka, and all Indigenous languages as "national languages" with legal protection.

But policy moved too slowly to outrun the losses.

A 2016 Hakka Affairs Council survey found approximately **4.53 million people** in Taiwan identify as Hakka, about **19.3%** of the population. Of them, only **46.8%** — roughly 2.12 million — can still speak Hakka. And that proportion is declining. The 2021 survey confirmed that younger generations show especially steep drops in Hakka listening and speaking ability.

Within a single generation, roughly half of all Hakka people in Taiwan lost their ancestral language.

## 570,000 Families Who Came by Marriage

Taiwan's ethnic story isn't finished.

Starting in the 1990s, large numbers of Taiwanese men began marrying women from Southeast Asia and mainland China. The media of that era called them "foreign brides," and news coverage was saturated with anxious narratives about "low educational quality," "language barriers," and "inability to raise children properly."

By 2024, Taiwan's **new immigrant population** had reached **570,000** (including mainland Chinese spouses). The largest source is mainland China (65.25%), followed by Vietnam (19.54%) and Indonesia (5.45%). These numbers don't include their Taiwan-born children — the "second-generation new immigrant" population has already surpassed one million.

Sociologist Lan Pei-chia at National Taipei University found that new immigrant children tend to adopt one of several identity strategies. The most common: emphasizing "my mother is Vietnamese, but I am Taiwanese" — a protective severance rather than genuine embrace. This distancing tactic is especially common among children who were bullied for their immigrant background growing up.

One Vietnamese mother, interviewed by the _United Daily News_ in 2024, told her children: "You are Taiwan's children, but remember Vietnam is your second home." She refused to let her children be trapped in the "new immigrant second-generation" label — but she also refused to pretend the label didn't exist.

The shift from "foreign bride" to "new immigrant" took twenty years to complete in official language. Those two terms represent two completely different ways of seeing the same person.

## Mainlanders: Forty-Five Provinces Under One Label

Between 1945 and the early 1950s, somewhere between one and two million people followed the ROC government to Taiwan — soldiers, officials, teachers, workers, and their families. They came from forty-five provinces and independent municipalities of mainland China, speaking Cantonese, Shandongese, Shanghainese, Hunanese.

They moved into military dependents' villages (眷村) scattered across Taiwan — temporary housing, designed for the interim period before "retaking the mainland." They stayed seventy years.

Today, the concept of "Mainlander" has blurred almost to invisibility among the third generation — intermarriage with Minnan and Hakka families, shared schools and workplaces and lives. But the cultural traces of eyun village life persist: the northern-style cuisine (beef noodles with ginger, hand-folded dumplings), elderly men speaking Mandarin with provincial accents at temple courtyards, and a fading emotional memory of Taiwan as somewhere between home and a temporary stop.

Mainlanders represent roughly 7.5% of Taiwan's population — but their role in shaping Taiwan's educational system and cultural infrastructure historically exceeded that proportion by a wide margin.

> **📝 Editor's Note**
> The "four major ethnic groups" framework has a political irony at its core: invented by the localization movement in 1993 to challenge Mainlander cultural dominance, it now, thirty years later, faces its own critiques. Ethnic categories create boundaries — but Taiwan's social reality is already one of intermarriage, mixed heritage, and multiple simultaneous identities. As Wang Fu-chang argues, ethnicity is "an ideology for categorizing people," not a fixed scientific taxonomy. Who decides where the boundaries are — and why there need to be boundaries at all — is a question each generation has to answer for itself.

## The Last Line of Defense: Language

Taiwan's [[語言多樣性與母語文化|language diversity and mother tongue crisis]] cuts across all ethnic groups equally.

The 1988 Return Our Mother Tongue march happened partly because, before that, speaking a mother tongue in a public setting was a punishable offense. After martial law ended, the prohibitions were gradually lifted. The 2019 National Language Development Act granted Taiwanese, Hakka, and all Indigenous languages formal legal recognition as national languages, placing them on school curricula.

But legal recognition cannot keep pace with the erosion already underway. The share of young people who speak fluent Taiwanese is declining. Of Taiwan's 16 officially recognized Indigenous languages, most are used daily only by aging elders. Hakka loses another generation of speakers roughly every decade.

Language death doesn't happen all at once. It's the accumulated effect of millions of family decisions: "Let's just speak Mandarin with the kids — it's more practical." Each individual decision is rational. The aggregate is irreversible.

Historical ethnic policy was enforced with weapons and law. Today's language loss is accomplished with convenience and choice. Compared to the violent suppression of the [[二二八事件|228 Incident]] and martial law era, this kind of disappearance is quieter — and much harder to see.

## What Can an Apology Do?

Whether the smoke from that burning millet stalk in 2016 was seen by the ancestors — that we can't know.

What is certain: the group held back by shields outside the Presidential Office did not have their land rights resolved by the apology document. Hakka language loss did not stop because Hakka TV started broadcasting. The way new immigrant mothers are treated at clinics, schools, and markets is not something a National Language Development Act can fix on its own.

In Taiwan, ethnicity is not a solved problem. Nor is it a problem that hasn't started being discussed. It's a problem that every generation has to renegotiate from scratch: who counts as "us," who gets left out, who owes what debts to history, and whether language will still be alive in the next generation to carry those questions forward.

Of those 614,000 Indigenous people, how many can still speak their mother tongue? Of those 570,000 new immigrant families' children, will they remember their mothers' languages when they grow up? For each Hakka speaker disappearing each year — whose child is it, and whose choice was it?

These numbers are still moving.

## Hakka Geographic Heartland

Hakka settlement in Taiwan follows a distinctive geographic logic: communities concentrated in the foothills, a band of terrain between the coastal plains (where Minnan settlers arrived first) and the mountains (where Indigenous peoples maintained their territories). This compressed geography shaped the community's character. "Hard-necked" (硬頸) — the Hakka self-description — wasn't just a cultural pose; it described actual conditions. Hillside farming, contested resources, and cultural pressures from a more populous neighbor all demanded resilience.

Today the Hakka heartland runs through Hsinchu County (71.6% Hakka) and Miaoli County (64.6%), extending into parts of Taoyuan (39.2%) and Hualien (31.7%). Taoyuan City has the single largest absolute Hakka population (785,000), followed by New Taipei City (549,000) and Taichung City (436,000).

A distinct subgroup — **Hoklo-Hakka** (福佬客) — are people of Hakka ancestry who shifted to Minnan as their primary language, concentrated in Yunlin, Changhua, Yilan, and Nantou. Their existence is itself a testament to the pressure Minnan cultural dominance has historically exerted.

## Minnan Dialects: Not One Group, But Three

The Minnan (Hoklo) community, Taiwan's largest at roughly 73.5%, is itself internally divided by ancestral origin. Quanzhou migrants (泉州人) settled primarily in the Taipei Basin and central coastal areas; Zhangzhou migrants (漳州人) took the central plains, northern coast, and Yilan Plain. The distinction still shows up in accent geography today:

- **Southern accent** (Zhangzhou-leaning): Kaohsiung, Tainan, Chiayi, Pingtung
- **Northern accent** (Quanzhou-leaning): Taipei, New Taipei, Keelung, parts of Taoyuan
- **Coastal accent**: Lukang, Taixi
- **Inland accent**: Yilan, Nantou

Chaozhou migrants from Guangdong's Chaoshan region were largely absorbed into the Minnan group due to linguistic proximity. The "four major ethnic groups" framework treats all these sub-communities as a single category — which works for census purposes, but flattens a lot of internal texture.
