# PTT: Taiwan's Most Stubborn Public Square

## 30-Second Overview

In 1995, a computer science sophomore at National Taiwan University named [[Tu Yi-Chin]] set up a BBS on a 486 PC in his dorm room and called it PTT (批踢踢實業坊). Thirty years later, this forum—still running on a plain-text telnet interface—has accumulated over 1.5 million registered accounts and peaked at more than 150,000 concurrent users. It gave birth to "villager" culture, reshaped Taiwan's media ecosystem, and played a pivotal role in the 2014 Sunflower Movement. Now, with Dcard and Threads rising, PTT confronts the reality of an aging user base.

---

## A 486 PC in a Dorm Room

In September 1995, Tu Yi-Chin installed Slackware Linux on a 486 computer in NTU's Male Dormitory 8 and launched PTT. University BBS boards were common in Taiwan at the time—NSYSU BBS at Sun Yat-sen University, BS2 at NCTU—but most were managed by computing centers and subject to institutional oversight.

Tu made a decision that seemed unremarkable at the time but proved defining: PTT would be student-governed, non-profit, and ad-free. That principle has held for thirty years.

By January 1997, PTT had just 581 registered users. But as broadband spread across Taiwan in the early 2000s, user numbers exploded.

---

## Push, Boo, Arrow: A Unique Rating System

PTT's most distinctive feature is its "push and boo" (推噓) system. Users can respond to any post with three actions: "push" (推, positive, displayed in red), "boo" (噓, negative, in blue), or "→" (neutral, grey arrow). These responses appear directly beneath the post, creating a real-time crowd verdict.

This was one of the earliest implementations of such a system in Chinese-language BBS, predating Reddit's upvote/downvote mechanism. Unlike Reddit, every push and boo on PTT is tied to a visible username—boo the wrong thing, and you'll get counter-booed. This accountability turned the comment section into its own layer of discourse. Sometimes the push thread is more interesting than the original post.

---

## Gossiping Board: Taiwan's Real-Time Opinion Thermometer

The Gossiping board (八卦板, established 1999) was originally created for celebrity gossip. It evolved into PTT's largest and most active board, maintaining over 5,000 concurrent users even during off-peak hours and surging past 10,000 during major events.

What made Gossiping significant was its role as Taiwan's real-time public reaction channel. Within minutes of any news event, the board would have first-hand accounts, "lazy packs" (懶人包—crowdsourced event summaries), and clashing viewpoints. Journalists mined it for story leads. Political staffers tracked it as a mood barometer. "Check PTT for the wind direction" was, for a long time, a reflexive habit for Taiwanese internet users.

PTT hosts over 20,000 boards in total, covering everything from Stock, NBA, and Baseball to Boy-Girl (relationships), marvel (paranormal stories), and joke. Each board has its own moderators, rules, and culture—some strict as the military (like C_Chat's spoiler enforcement), others chaotic as a night market.

## How PTT Changed Taiwan's Language

PTT's most enduring impact on Taiwan may be linguistic. Numerous PTT terms have infiltrated everyday speech, used naturally even by people who have never logged in:

"**Villager**" (鄉民)—PTT users' self-designation, originating from a line in Stephen Chow's 1994 film *Hail the Judge* (九品芝麻官): "I'm just a villager who came to watch the show." In 2004, a PTT administrator told rubberneckers to "please step behind the yellow line, villagers"—the term stuck, evolving from mockery into a badge of identity. "**Fifth floor**" (五樓)—the fifth comment in a push thread, which in the early days often contained a brilliant response, spawning the meme "fifth floor, please speak." "**+1**"—agreement, a one-character show of support. "**Leading the wind direction**" (帶風向)—organized attempts to manufacture opinion on forums, which crossed over from PTT slang into mainstream Taiwanese vocabulary.

"Celestial dragon people" (天龍人, mocking Taipei residents' perceived elitism), "paid placement" (業配, sponsored content disguised as organic), "lazy pack" (懶人包, complex issues distilled into digestible summaries)—these terms have long outgrown PTT itself.

## Sunflower Movement, Elections, and Fake News

During the 2014 Sunflower Movement, PTT became the protesters' primary hub for information coordination and mobilization. Real-time updates, supply requests, and legal advice all flowed through the platform. The movement demonstrated that a BBS from 1995 could still punch above its weight in a social crisis.

Every election season, the Gossiping board and HatePolitics board (政黑板) turn into political battlegrounds. Partisans clash in push-and-boo wars, PR firms are accused of buying accounts to "lead the wind direction," and moderators struggle to process reports and bans. PTT's political discourse runs hot with energy—and smoke.

In 2018, PTT announced it would suspend new account applications, partly because a flood of fake accounts had been implicated in election-related disinformation. The decision improved account quality but effectively shut the door on a new generation of younger users.

---

## The Love-Hate Relationship with the Media

Taiwanese media has a complicated relationship with PTT. On one hand, journalists constantly mine the platform for stories—"PTT villagers are buzzing about..." became a lazy headline formula. On the other, PTT users despise this practice, calling reporters who copy-paste forum posts "keyboard journalists" (鍵盤記者).

This dynamic created a peculiar media loop: PTT discussions get amplified by news coverage, which flows back to PTT where it gets dissected and critiqued by users. Some events became news *only because* they went viral on PTT. Some news stories lost credibility *because* PTT users called them out.

---

## A Thirty-Year-Old BBS Facing Reality

PTT is getting older. The 2018 registration freeze, a 2023 email verification sweep that locked out masses of unverified accounts, and younger users migrating to Dcard (an anonymous college forum) and Threads (which exploded in Taiwan from 2023)—all have contributed to declining active users.

The plain-text interface was once PTT's point of pride: fast loading, censorship-resistant, free from algorithmic manipulation. But for a generation raised on smartphones, a forum without images or video is an alien world. Mobile apps like MoPTT and PiTT provide mobile access, yet the core experience remains text-only.

The rise of Threads in Taiwan has been particularly impactful. When news breaks, many Taiwanese users' first instinct has shifted from "check PTT for the wind direction" to "check Tsui (Threads) for what people are saying."

---

## But PTT Is Still Here

Even as activity declines, PTT retains irreplaceable niches. The Stock board still buzzes during trading hours. The Baseball board fills up during CPBL and WBC seasons. Gossiping and HatePolitics remain central political arenas during elections.

What makes PTT truly irreplaceable is its thirty-year archive of discussions. Want to read the raw public reaction to a social controversy from five years ago? Looking for honest product reviews from a decade back? PTT's historical posts form one of the most complete records of Taiwan's collective internet memory.

Tu Yi-Chin left Microsoft in 2017 to return to Taiwan and found [Taiwan AI Labs](https://ailabs.tw/), but PTT is still maintained by NTU student volunteers. It remains non-profit. It still runs no ads. In an era where every platform chases engagement metrics and monetization, the fact that a thirty-year-old BBS can still hold to these principles is itself a story worth telling.
