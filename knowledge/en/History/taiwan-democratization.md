# Taiwan Democratization

> **30-second overview:** In December 1979, 29-year-old Chen Chu was dragged onto a police van on the streets of Kaohsiung. In prison, she wrote a farewell letter, expecting to be executed. Forty-one years later, she became president of the Control Yuan — Taiwan's highest government oversight body. Taiwan's democratization was not a switch flipped overnight. It was a forty-year experiment: from the world's longest martial law (38 years and 56 days) to one of Asia's freest democracies (Freedom House 93/100 in 2025, 6th globally). No revolution. No coup. No civil war.

On March 18, 1980, a military court in Taiwan opened the trial of the [[Kaohsiung Incident]] defendants. In the dock: Shih Ming-teh, Huang Hsin-chieh, Chen Chu, and Annette Lu. The gallery was packed with foreign correspondents. US Senator Ted Kennedy entered Shih's case into the Congressional Record.

The KMT intended the public trial to demonstrate judicial legitimacy. It backfired spectacularly.

The trial gave the defendants a national stage. Shih had prepared a 60,000-character defense statement. Then he learned that [[Lin Yi-hsiung]]'s mother and twin daughters had been murdered outside his home while Lin was in custody. The [[Lin Family Massacre|林宅血案]] — the family of an imprisoned political opponent, attacked in broad daylight. Shih put down the defense statement and asked the judge to sentence him to death. The image traveled across Taiwan.

> 📝 **Curator's note:** The most absurd consequence of the Kaohsiung trial was what happened to the defense lawyers. [[Chen Shui-bian]], Hsieh Chang-ting, Su Tseng-chang, Chang Chun-hsiung, Yu Ching — nearly all became president, premier, or mayor of a major city within twenty years. The KMT's carefully staged show trial inadvertently incubated an entire generation of opposition leadership.

## Nylon Cheng's Seventy-One Days

In the decade after the Kaohsiung trial, Taiwanese society was water slowly heating toward a boil. The opposition movement kept expanding — but the person who pushed the temperature to the breaking point was a mainlander's son named [[Cheng Nan-jung]], known by his English nickname Nylon Cheng.

On December 10, 1988 — International Human Rights Day — Cheng published the full text of a draft constitution for a "Republic of Taiwan" in issue 254 of his _Freedom Era Weekly_. Under existing law, this was sedition. Punishable by death.

After receiving a court summons, Cheng barricaded himself inside the magazine office and refused to appear. His last words to his wife Yeh Chu-lan:

**"The rest is up to you all."**

On April 7, 1989, at 7:30 a.m., nearly 200 officers surrounded the building. At 9:15 a.m., Cheng Nan-jung set himself on fire. He was 41. Every issue of his magazine had carried the same line on its back cover: **"Fight for 100% freedom of speech!"**

On May 19, tens of thousands marched in the rain, following his coffin toward the Presidential Office.

> 📝 **Curator's note:** The SWAT commander who led the raid was Hou You-yi. Thirty-five years later, in 2024, Hou ran for president as the KMT candidate. The same person who stood on the side of state violence at Taiwan's most brutal moment of democratization then competed for the highest office in the democratic system he once helped suppress. That fact alone captures the absurdity and complexity of Taiwan's history.

Cheng's death directly accelerated the 1992 revision of Criminal Code Article 100 and the abolition of the Punishment for Sedition Act. Thought was no longer a crime. April 7 is now officially "Freedom of Speech Day."

## Martial Law's End Was Not Freedom's Start

On July 14, 1987, 75-year-old [[Chiang Ching-kuo]] held a press conference at the Presidential Office and announced that martial law would lift at midnight. Twelve days later, he invited twelve local elders to the Presidential Office for tea and offered a sentence that has been selectively quoted across the political spectrum ever since:

**"I have lived in Taiwan for forty years. I am Taiwanese, and of course also Chinese."**

Independence-leaning voices cite the first half. Unification advocates emphasize the second. In 1987, the sentence served a precise social function: a mainlander strongman acknowledging he too was Taiwanese, trying to ease ethnic tensions before the coming political opening.

Chiang died six months later.

But ending martial law did not mean freedom had arrived. The government simultaneously passed the National Security Act. Article 9 stipulated that military court verdicts against civilians during the martial law era "shall not be appealed." White Terror victims were legally blocked from any remedy against the judgments that had imprisoned or exiled them.

> **"Some scholars argue that Taiwan's real democratization began not in 1987 but in 1992 — when Criminal Code Article 100 was revised and the Punishment for Sedition Act abolished. Only then was freedom of thought legally protected."**

This is why some people who lived through the martial law years remember July 15, 1987 not as liberation but as bewilderment. You could hold a demonstration now — but who dared? You could form a political party — but would you be arrested for trying? Democracy is not a light you switch on. It requires an entire society to relearn, slowly and painfully, how not to be afraid.

## Six Thousand Wild Lilies vs. Tiananmen

On March 16, 1990 — less than nine months after the Tiananmen Square massacre — students from National Taiwan University began a sit-in at the Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall plaza. The immediate trigger: [[Lee Teng-hui]] needed re-election as president, but the body voting on the matter was the National Assembly elected on the Chinese mainland in 1947. Over 700 representatives who had never faced voters again in over four decades — what students called the "[[Wansui Congress|萬年國會]]," the eternal parliament.

A banner read: **"Countrymen, how can we tolerate the oppression of 700 emperors?"**

From a handful of students, the sit-in grew to nearly 6,000 within six days. They held wild lilies — a flower native to Taiwan's mountains, symbolizing purity — and made four demands: dissolve the National Assembly, abolish the Temporary Provisions, convene a National Affairs Conference, set a timetable for reform.

On March 21, Lee Teng-hui received fifty student representatives at the Presidential Office. He reportedly issued a standing order that students were not to be harmed.

This was Tiananmen's mirror image. Nine months earlier, Beijing's students received tanks. Taipei's students received the president's promise. The promises were kept:

- May 1991: Temporary Provisions abolished
- December 1991: The "eternal parliament" dissolved
- 1992: Full Legislative Yuan elections held
- March 23, 1996: Taiwan held its first direct presidential election

> 📝 **Curator's note:** The Wild Lily student leaders' life trajectories read like a political encyclopedia of the next generation: Fan Yun became a DPP legislator and feminist activist, Lin Chia-lung became Minister of Foreign Affairs, Lo Wen-chia rose to the DPP's executive inner circle, Cheng Wen-tsan became mayor of Taoyuan. The movement didn't just change institutions — it forged the political identity of an entire cohort.

## Voting Under Missiles

In March 1996, on the eve of Taiwan's first presidential election, the People's Liberation Army test-fired missiles into waters 20 miles off Keelung and 29 miles off Kaohsiung, disrupting roughly 70% of commercial shipping through those ports. The PLA simultaneously massed an estimated 100,000 troops along the Fujian coast.

Beijing's intent was clear: frighten voters away from [[Lee Teng-hui]].

The United States responded by dispatching the USS Nimitz and USS Independence carrier battle groups — the largest American naval deployment in the Pacific since Vietnam.

On March 23, turnout was 76.04%. Lee won 5,813,699 votes — 54% of the total. Analysts estimated that Beijing's intimidation campaign may have driven roughly 5 percentage points of voters toward Lee, converting what might have been a plurality into a clear majority.

Beijing's carefully planned coercion produced exactly the result it was designed to prevent.

## Three Transfers of Power: The Discipline of Losing

The real test of democracy is not elections. It is whether the losing party walks out of the palace.

**2000:** Chen Shui-bian won with 39.3%, ending 55 years of uninterrupted KMT rule. It was the first peaceful transfer of power through elections in any Chinese-speaking society. The result was made possible by a KMT split: James Soong's independent candidacy drew 37%, leaving official KMT nominee Lien Chan with 23%.

**2008:** Ma Ying-jeou defeated Hsieh Chang-ting 58.45% to 41.55%. The DPP handed over power without incident, demonstrating that conceding defeat was not a KMT institutional habit but a democratic norm capable of crossing party lines.

**2016:** Tsai Ing-wen defeated Chu Li-luan with 56.12%. The KMT left office again, peacefully.

**2024:** Lai Ching-te won with 40.05% — a plurality, not a majority — as the DPP governed for the first time without a legislative majority while opposition parties controlled the legislature. Another stress test for democratic resilience, with institutions holding.

Four transfers of power across thirty years. Each peaceful. No coups. No refusal to concede. No political violence at the transition.

## Why No Bloodshed?

Eastern Europe's communist regimes fell to revolution. The Arab Spring collapsed into civil war. Myanmar's generals drowned their democracy in blood. Why did Taiwan's transition remain, as observers have called it, a "quiet revolution"?

Political scientist Samuel Huntington classified Taiwan in _The Third Wave_ as a "transformation" — elite-led, top-down democratization — distinct from South Korea's negotiated "transplacement" and the Philippines' mass-driven "replacement" at the 1986 EDSA People Power Revolution.

But why did elites choose reform rather than repression? Three structural conditions made the difference:

**The economic miracle created a middle class with something to lose.** Between 1960 and 1990, Taiwan's per capita GDP surged from $164 to $8,111. A middle class with savings, property, and children enrolled in schools does not gamble these on revolution. They want reform — orderly, negotiated, preserving what has been built.

**International isolation forced the regime to find domestic legitimacy.** Expelled from the United Nations in 1971. Abandoned by the United States in 1979. Stripped of Olympic membership. Once the KMT could no longer justify its rule by promising to retake the mainland, it had no legitimating story left except performance for the people actually living under it. Democratic opening was, among other things, a survival strategy.

**The opposition chose institutional paths at every critical juncture.** The Formosa movement and the DPP consistently opted for legal and electoral resistance over violent confrontation. The 1986 EDSA Revolution in Manila demonstrated that peaceful regime change was achievable — a proof of concept that emboldened Taiwan's own reformers to believe gradualism could win.

## An Imperfect Democracy

Freedom House awarded Taiwan 93/100 in 2025: 6th globally, 2nd in Asia after Japan (96). The Economist's Democracy Index ranked Taiwan 12th in 2024, with a perfect score of 10 for electoral process and pluralism — the only "full democracy" in Asia by that measure.

But numbers contain fractures.

During the [[台灣白色恐怖|White Terror]], an estimated 140,000 to 200,000 people were politically persecuted. Between 3,000 and 4,000 were executed. Transitional justice remains formally incomplete. National Security Act Article 9, which blocked legal remedy for White Terror victims, was not declared unconstitutional until 2019 — thirty-two years after martial law officially ended.

Disinformation has become the new battlefield. The 2018 local elections and 2020 presidential election both saw large-scale disinformation campaigns, with significant portions traced to foreign-origin sources. Freedom House's 2025 report documented ongoing Chinese government efforts to shape Taiwan's policy environment, media landscape, and democratic infrastructure.

Partisan polarization degrades policy quality. Major legislation is frequently stalled or forced through on party-line votes bearing no relationship to the merits of the underlying policy.

Taiwan's democracy is not perfect. But it is functioning. And it is alive.

## Chen Chu's Farewell Letter

In December 1979, 29-year-old Chen Chu wrote a farewell letter in prison, expecting to be executed. She said goodbye to the people of Taiwan.

She was not executed. She served six years. After release, she helped found the Democratic Progressive Party. She served as Taipei's Director of Social Affairs, then chaired the Council of Labor Affairs, then governed Kaohsiung for twelve years. In 2014 she was re-elected with 990,000 votes — the highest total for any local executive that year. In 2020, she was nominated as president of the Control Yuan, Taiwan's highest government oversight body.

From farewell letter to Control Yuan president: forty-one years.

Same person. Same island. The only difference: in 1979, what she thought was a crime. In 2020, the former political prisoner was in charge of investigating whether the government committed crimes.

That is what democratization means. Not a smooth inspirational arc but a political experiment thick with absurdity, contradiction, and irreversible cost. The people who started it did not know it would work. Many expected to die. The experiment is still running.
