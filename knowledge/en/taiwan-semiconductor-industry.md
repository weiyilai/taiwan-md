# Taiwan Semiconductor Industry

> **30-second overview:** TSMC begins 2-nanometer mass production in late 2025, leading globally by 2-3 years. The 3nm process utilization has reached 100%, with advanced processes accounting for 69% of revenue. From Morris Chang's foundry model gamble in 1987 to today's global tech lifeline—Taiwan controls the world's most advanced chip manufacturing technology.

One afternoon in 1985, Minister Li Kuo-ting approached Morris Chang, who had just returned to Taiwan to become president of the Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI). Li got straight to the point: "We want to establish a large-scale integrated circuit manufacturing company, and we want you to lead it."

Chang was stunned. He thought he was just coming back to be an institute president, but two weeks later, he found himself founding a company with a business model nobody had ever tried.

That conversation changed the world.

## 1987: An Unprecedented Gamble

### The RCA Technology Transfer Prelude

The story begins in 1973, when ITRI spent $4.5 million to acquire integrated circuit technology from RCA Corporation, sending 19 engineers to the United States for training. Nobody imagined at the time that this "tuition fee" would become the cornerstone of Taiwan's semiconductor empire.

In 1980, ITRI spun off United Microelectronics Corporation (UMC) through technology transfer, giving Taiwan its first semiconductor company. But Li Kuo-ting wasn't satisfied—UMC was too small, its technology couldn't match international standards. Taiwan needed a bigger breakthrough.

### Morris Chang's Radical Vision

On February 21, 1987, Morris Chang established Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) in Hsinchu Science Park, pioneering an unprecedented business model: **pure-play foundry**.

The idea sounded insane at the time. Every semiconductor company in the world was vertically integrated—from design to manufacturing in one operation. How could you only manufacture without designing? Would customers really hand over their most confidential designs to you?

Chang's logic was simple: the semiconductor industry was becoming increasingly complex, and design and manufacturing were two completely different specialties. Rather than doing everything mediocrely, why not focus on one thing and do it better than anyone else in the world—chip manufacturing.

### The Ingenious International Partnership

TSMC's initial shareholding structure was ingenious:

- Government investment: 48.3% (ensuring national strategic position)
- Private investment: 24.2% (Taiwan capital participation)
- Netherlands Philips: 27.5% (bringing international technology and customers)

Philips' participation was crucial. At the time, the semiconductor industry was dominated by the U.S. and Japan, and Europe desperately needed alternative suppliers. Philips not only invested but also gave TSMC their own chip orders, becoming the first major customer.

## The Foundry Model: Reshaping Global Semiconductor Ecology

### Industrial Division Revolution

TSMC's foundry model triggered massive specialization in the semiconductor industry:

- **IC design companies** focus on chip design (Qualcomm, NVIDIA, MediaTek)
- **Foundries** focus on chip manufacturing (TSMC, UMC, GlobalFoundries)
- **Assembly and test** handle back-end processes (ASE Group, SPIL)

This model allowed more companies to enter the semiconductor industry. Previously, only giants like Intel and IBM could afford the astronomical investment of fabs. Now any startup with a good idea could design chips and have TSMC manufacture them.

### Building Trust Mechanisms

The foundry model's core is trust. Customers must believe TSMC won't steal their designs, leak trade secrets, or compete with them.

TSMC established strict "trust rules":

1. **Technology neutrality**: Never design chips in-house
2. **Customer equality**: All customers receive the same technology and service
3. **Confidentiality agreements**: Highest-level information security protection
4. **Capacity allocation**: Fair distribution of advanced process capacity

These rules have been enforced for nearly 40 years without exception.

## Process Technology: The Eternal Arms Race

### Chasing Moore's Law

In 1965, Intel co-founder Gordon Moore proposed "Moore's Law": the number of transistors on a chip doubles every 18 months. This law has driven the semiconductor industry's development for over 50 years and became the track for TSMC's competition with global rivals.

Starting from 3-micrometer process in 1987, TSMC caught up step by step:

- **1990s**: 1.2μm, 0.8μm, 0.6μm
- **2000s**: 0.35μm, 0.25μm, 0.18μm, 0.13μm, 90nm
- **2010s**: 65nm, 40nm, 28nm, 20nm, 16nm, 10nm, 7nm
- **2020s**: 5nm, 3nm, 2nm

### The Arrival of the 2-Nanometer Era

In Q4 2025, TSMC began 2-nanometer process mass production, with main production lines at Hsinchu Baoshan Fab 20 and Kaohsiung Fab 22. This represents a new limit of human manufacturing technology—2 nanometers equals the diameter of 20 atoms, approaching the theoretical boundaries of physics.

TSMC estimates that by the end of 2026, 2nm process monthly capacity will reach 100,000 wafers, with first customers including Apple's A-series chips and NVIDIA's AI chips.

What makes competitors even more desperate: TSMC is already developing 1.6nm (A16) process, expected for mass production in the second half of 2026. While the world is still catching up to 3nm, TSMC is already sprinting toward the angstrom level (sub-1nm).

### The Cost of Technology Leadership

Advanced process R&D costs show exponential growth:

- 28nm process: $1 billion
- 7nm process: $3 billion
- 3nm process: $10 billion
- 2nm process: Estimated over $20 billion

These astronomical investments mean fewer and fewer companies can keep up with this game. Currently, only three companies worldwide remain in the advanced process arms race: TSMC, Samsung, and Intel—and TSMC leads the other two by 2-3 generations.

## The Silicon Shield: A New Geopolitical Bargaining Chip

### The Silicon Shield Effect

TSMC's technological advantage has unexpectedly become Taiwan's "silicon shield" in geopolitics. Tech giants worldwide depend on TSMC's advanced processes:

- **Apple**: iPhone, iPad, Mac core chips
- **NVIDIA**: AI training and inference chips
- **AMD**: CPU and GPU chips
- **Qualcomm**: 5G smartphone chips
- **Tesla**: Self-driving chips

If TSMC stopped production for a week, the global tech supply chain would collapse. This strategic position gives Taiwan unprecedented influence in international politics.

### The Global Scramble

Because TSMC is so important, every country wants to bring this technology home:

**U.S. Arizona Fab** (2025-2028):
$40 billion investment, expected to produce 5nm and 3nm chips. But capacity is limited—more symbolic than practical significance.

**Japan Kumamoto Fab** (started production 2024):
Partnering with Sony and Toyota to produce 22nm to 28nm automotive chips. Not the most advanced processes, but sufficient for Japan's automotive industry needs.

**Germany Dresden Fab** (2027 operation):
Partnering with BMW and Bosch, targeting the European automotive electronics market.

But the key to these overseas fabs is: **the most advanced process technologies remain in Taiwan**. 2nm, 1.6nm and other cutting-edge technologies won't be transferred overseas in the short term. Taiwan remains the heart of global semiconductor technology.

## AI Wave: An Unexpected Second Spring

### The Shift from Mobile to AI

Before 2020, TSMC's main customers were smartphone chip manufacturers. But after ChatGPT's explosive popularity in 2022, AI became the new growth engine.

AI training and inference require massive computational power, driving explosive demand for high-end chips. NVIDIA's H100 and H200 AI chips are all produced using TSMC's 4nm and 5nm processes. Apple's M-series chips also added more AI capabilities.

2024 data shows advanced processes (7nm and more advanced) revenue reached 69% of TSMC's total wafer sales. This percentage continues rising.

### CoWoS Packaging Technology Breakthrough

AI chips need to process massive amounts of data; single chips are no longer sufficient—multiple chips must be "stacked" together. TSMC's CoWoS (Chip on Wafer on Substrate) packaging technology has become a key technology in the AI era.

NVIDIA's H100 chips use CoWoS technology to package GPU chips with high-speed memory together. This technology has extremely high barriers, and currently only TSMC can mass-produce it.

## Competitors: Samsung's Chase and Intel's Struggle

### Samsung's Korean Offensive

South Korea's Samsung is TSMC's main competitor, adopting a completely different strategy:

- **Vertical integration**: From memory, chip foundry to smartphone manufacturing
- **Massive investment**: $230 billion investment from 2022-2026
- **Government support**: Korea's "K-Semiconductor Belt" plan provides full support

But Samsung still lags behind TSMC in advanced process yields. In 2023, Samsung's 3nm process yield was about 60%, while TSMC achieved over 90%. In the foundry industry where precision determines everything, yield differences mean worlds apart.

### Intel's Transformation Struggles

Intel was once the global semiconductor technology leader, but got stuck on 10nm process for years, missing the best opportunity to compete with TSMC.

In 2021, Intel proposed the "IDM 2.0" strategy to operate both in-house chip design and foundry businesses simultaneously. But by 2025, Intel's foundry business still hasn't secured major customers. Most ironically, Intel's own designed high-end chips are now manufactured by TSMC.

## Taiwan's Semiconductor Ecosystem: More Than Just TSMC

### Complete Supply Chain Advantages

Taiwan's semiconductor industry competitiveness comes not just from TSMC, but from the entire ecosystem:

**IC Design**: MediaTek (global top 3), Novatek, Realtek, Himax
**Wafer Foundry**: TSMC (global #1), UMC, Vanguard
**Assembly & Test**: ASE Group (global #1), SPIL, ChipMOS
**Equipment & Materials**: Nanya Technology, Winbond, WIN Semi

This complete industrial cluster creates powerful synergy effects. A chip can go from design to completion by making a circuit around Taiwan, without international shipping.

### Talent Convergence in Hsinchu Science Park

Established in 1980, Hsinchu Science Park has become one of the world's most important semiconductor clusters after 40+ years of development. The park houses over 500 companies with more than 170,000 employees.

More importantly is talent flow. Engineers might work at TSMC for a few years, then jump to MediaTek to design chips, then move to ASE Group for packaging. This talent circulation continuously elevates the technical level of the entire industry.

## Future Challenges: Physical Limits and Geopolitical Risks

### The End of Moore's Law

2nm is already approaching the physical limits of silicon-based semiconductors. To continue shrinking, new materials (like carbon nanotubes, graphene) or new architectures (like quantum computing, photonic computing) may be needed.

TSMC's research on "angstrom-level" processes (sub-1nm) faces exponentially increasing technical difficulty. The cost per transistor might actually increase, challenging traditional economic logic.

### The Double-Edged Sword of Geopolitics

TSMC's strategic position is both protection and risk. The U.S.-China tech war puts Taiwan in the middle, requiring balance between two superpowers:

- **U.S. pressure**: Requiring TSMC to comply with technology sanctions on China
- **Chinese market**: Still an important revenue source for TSMC
- **Supply chain risk**: Over-concentration of production in Taiwan

### The Talent War

The world is competing for semiconductor talent. The U.S. CHIPS Act, EU Chips Act, and Japan's semiconductor strategy all offer massive subsidies to attract talent. TSMC must maintain competitiveness in the global talent market.

## 2030 Vision: Beyond Moore's Law

### The New Era of Heterogeneous Integration

When transistor shrinking becomes impossible, the semiconductor industry turns to "heterogeneous integration"—integrating chips with different functions in the same package:

- **Processor chips**: Handle computation
- **Memory chips**: Handle storage
- **Sensor chips**: Handle sensing
- **RF chips**: Handle communication

TSMC's 3D IC technology allows chips to be "vertically stacked," cramming more functionality into limited area.

### The Quantum Computing Challenge

Google, IBM, IonQ and other companies are developing quantum computing, claiming it can solve complex problems traditional computers cannot handle. If quantum computing truly breaks through, it might disrupt the entire semiconductor industry.

But TSMC is also positioning itself. The company is collaborating with IBM on quantum computing manufacturing technology, preparing for the next tech revolution.

## Conclusion: The Eternal Race of Technology Island

Starting from Morris Chang's adventure in 1987, TSMC spent nearly 40 years transforming Taiwan into the lifeline of global technology. This is not just one company's success, but the strategic miracle of an entire island.

Today, when you send messages on iPhone, ask ChatGPT questions, or activate Tesla's autopilot, you're actually using Taiwan's technology. 69% of the world's most advanced chips are manufactured on this small island.

But how long can this advantage last? Technology leadership has no endpoint—every day is a new beginning. TSMC must continuously invest, innovate, and lead to maintain its position in this eternal tech competition.

For Taiwan, the semiconductor industry is not just an economic pillar, but a survival strategy in the globalized era. When geopolitics becomes increasingly complex, technological advantage might be a small nation's most powerful protection.

Taiwan's story is still being written. And the next chapter will be the challenge of angstrom-level processes.
