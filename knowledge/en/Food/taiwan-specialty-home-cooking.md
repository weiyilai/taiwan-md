> **30-Second Overview**: "Shǒulù cài" (手路菜) in Taiwanese means "specialty dishes," referring to those time-consuming, skill-demanding family recipes passed down through generations. From Chiayi's mint chicken to Tainan's soup made from banquet scraps (càiwěi tāng), these dishes carrying family memories are gradually disappearing in the wave of modernization, becoming eternal nostalgia for the older generation.

"My grandmother's mint chicken—you couldn't find another like it in all of Chiayi." Jenny L.'s eyes filled with nostalgia as she shared her family story. The mint chicken she spoke of isn't something you'd find in regular restaurants, but a specialty home dish unique to the Chiayi region—fresh mint leaves stir-fried with chicken, refreshingly aromatic, a summer comfort food.

But today, fewer and fewer people know how to make this dish. After Jenny L.'s grandmother passed away, this unique flavor vanished with her, becoming an irretrievable regret in the family's collective memory.

## What Are Specialty Home Dishes (Shǒulù Cài)?

"Shǒulù cài" is a Taiwanese term that, according to Taiwan's Ministry of Education Taiwanese Dictionary, means "specialty dishes." But in Taiwan's culinary culture, specialty home cooking means far more than the literal definition—it refers to dishes that require skill, are time-consuming and labor-intensive, and often carry family heritage.

These dishes cannot be casually made. They require "shǒulù" (skill)—unique cooking techniques, precise control of heat, deep understanding of ingredients, and most importantly—accumulated time and passed-down experience.

A true specialty home dish is often a family's or region's "secret recipe," with flavors that others cannot learn or buy elsewhere.

## Vanishing Family Memories

Like Jenny L.'s Chiayi mint chicken, many specialty home dishes are quietly disappearing during Taiwan society's modernization. This phenomenon has complex social reasons behind it.

First is the change in lifestyle. In the past, Taiwanese families made cooking an important part of the day, with grandmothers having ample time to simmer labor-intensive dishes. Modern life moves at a faster pace, with dining-out culture flourishing, and fewer people willing to spend hours preparing a single dish.

Second is the break in transmission. Many specialty dishes lack standardized recipes, relying on "learning by watching" and "learning by doing" in a master-apprentice model. When the younger generation no longer has patience to learn, or when the older generation doesn't have time to pass down knowledge, these skills are lost to time.

## Banquet Culture: The Last Fortress

In Taiwan's specialty home cooking culture, "bànzhuō" (outdoor banquet) represents the last fortress. Master chefs possess many skills on the verge of extinction, most notably represented by "càiwěi tāng" (leftover soup).

Many people mistakenly think leftover soup is simply a mixture of leftover dishes cooked together, but this understanding is wrong. According to Taiwanese cuisine expert Huang Wan-ling, leftover soup is the final dish in Taiwanese banquets and the ultimate test of a master chef's skill.

An authentic leftover soup requires pre-saving portions from seven dishes: white radish and pork belly soup, bamboo shoot and pickled vegetable pork rib soup, braised thick soup, braised pork, fish ball soup, and wǔliǔzhī (five-willow fish), calculating precise proportions before "combining" (jié) them into one pot. The Taiwanese word "jié" means to harmonize and fuse—this cooking method is quite rare across all cuisine styles.

A pot of leftover soup takes over four hours from preparation to completion, testing not only technique but also the ability to control the rhythm of an entire banquet.

## Regional Specialty Home Dishes

Each region of Taiwan has its own unique specialty dishes, reflecting different areas' ingredient characteristics and cultural backgrounds.

**Red-claw crab with glutinous rice** is a classic Tainan specialty. Fresh red-claw crab paired with glutinous rice requires precise steaming time control, allowing the crab roe's umami to completely permeate the rice grains. While seemingly simple, this dish demands extremely high standards for ingredient freshness and heat control.

**Hakka preserved radish omelet** appears ordinary but tests one's skill. The pickling time of preserved radish (càipú), cutting method, and ratio with eggs—each step affects the final taste. Authentic Hakka grandmothers' preserved radish omelets have unique layers of salty fragrance that restaurant versions struggle to replicate.

**Penghu's fried dates** are essential New Year specialty dishes. These seemingly simple glutinous rice dumplings actually have strict requirements for oil temperature, dough moisture, and frying time. Each family's fried dates have subtle differences, carrying different family memories.

## Modern Revival Efforts

Facing the crisis of disappearing specialty home dishes, some dedicated individuals have begun revival work.

Taiwanese cuisine expert Huang Wan-ling dedicates herself to discovering and documenting traditional Taiwanese cuisine. Her "Huang Wan-ling Cooking School" specializes in teaching nearly extinct specialty dishes. She says: "The loss of a leftover soup recipe represents not just the disappearance of a recipe, but the fracture of an entire cultural context."

Some restaurants have also begun featuring "replica specialty home dishes," attempting to recreate grandmothers' era flavors. Taipei restaurants like "Feng He Ri Li" and "Taipei Hospitality" use traditional specialty dishes as selling points, giving younger generations opportunities to taste these nearly extinct flavors.

But replicas are ultimately not the original taste. For standardization and cost considerations, restaurants often adjust original methods. True specialty home dishes carry not only flavors but also the emotions and memories of their makers.

## Cultural Value of Specialty Home Dishes

The value of specialty home dishes lies not only in taste but in their cultural significance. Each specialty dish represents a family's history, a region's memory, an era's imprint.

When we lose these specialty dishes, we lose more than food—we lose a way of life, a cultural tradition, an emotional connection between people. In that era without frozen foods or delivery platforms, specialty home dishes symbolized family warmth and were concrete embodiments of maternal love.

## New Possibilities for Transmission

Although traditional master-apprentice transmission faces challenges in modern society, new technologies also bring possibilities for preserving specialty home dishes.

Some people have begun using videos to record grandmothers' cooking processes and writing down specialty dish recipes. Social media allows scattered hometown people to exchange methods for hometown flavors. Some have even developed smartphone apps specifically for collecting and sharing regional specialty dish recipes.

More importantly, increasing numbers of young people are becoming aware of the preciousness of these taste memories. They actively learn from elders, using modern methods to record and transmit these nearly extinct skills.

## Searching for Lost Flavors

Perhaps one day, when we miss a certain specialty dish from grandma, we won't only be able to search for it in memory. Perhaps these dishes carrying family memories can return to our dining tables through the efforts of new generations.

The story of specialty home dishes is actually every Taiwanese person's story. In rapidly changing times, we're all searching for those lost flavors, searching for those gradually fading memories. And when we learn to treasure these seemingly ordinary home-cooked dishes again, perhaps that's the beginning of finding our roots.

As Jenny L. said: "I've always thought, if I had seriously learned grandma's mint chicken back then, I could make it for my children now."

This sentence expresses the regret in so many people's hearts and points to the true meaning of specialty home dish transmission—not just the continuation of skills, but the passing down of love.
