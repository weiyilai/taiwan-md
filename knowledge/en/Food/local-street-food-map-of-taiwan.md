# Local Street Food Map of Taiwan

> "The most beautiful scenery in Taiwan is its people; the deepest culture lies in its street food. In the depths of every city's alleys, there are taste codes that belong exclusively to this land."

On the beautiful island of Taiwan, every city has its own culinary identity card. Not those chain stores or tourist restaurants, but the street food stalls rooted locally and passed down through generations—these truly represent the soul of each place.

These street foods often have humble appearances yet contain profound cultural heritage. They might be innovations from early immigrants adapting to new environments, fusions of local ingredients with foreign techniques, or delicious traces left by historical events. Following this local street food map, we're not just seeking delicacies; we're exploring the unique cultural landscapes of various parts of Taiwan.

## 🗺️ Northern Region: Local Persistence Under International Vision

### Keelung City: Seafood and Human Warmth of the Port City

**Representative dishes: Ding-bian-hu (鼎邊糊), crab shell pastry, bubble ice**

Keelung Temple Mouth Night Market is probably one of Taiwan's most historically rich night markets. Walking on Ren San Road in front of Dianji Temple, you'll discover that the street food here carries the strong flavors of the sea and port city characteristics.

**Ding-bian-hu** is Keelung people's pride. This dish originating from Fuzhou developed its unique flavor in Keelung's harbor environment. Rice paste forms thin sheets along the pot's edge, paired with meat soup, shrimp, and chives—this fusion of seafood freshness and rice fragrance is a taste unique to Keelung. The old stall A-Hua Ding-bian-hu has been passed down for three generations, maintaining the most authentic port city flavors in every bowl.

**Crab shell pastry** showcases Keelung people's love for crispy delicacies. This baked pastry, golden like a crab shell, is crispy outside and soft inside, filled with pork shreds or red bean paste—a delicious memory for Keelung people from childhood to adulthood.

### Taipei City: Confluence of Eight Great Cuisines

**Representative dishes: Da chang bao xiao chang (大腸包小腸, Taiwanese hot dog), pepper bun, pan-fried buns**

As Taiwan's capital, Taipei brings together culinary cultures from all regions. Even in this international metropolis, there are many authentic Taipei flavors.

**Da chang bao xiao chang** is a classic creation of Taipei night markets. Glutinous rice sausage (da chang) wrapped around Taiwanese sausage (xiao chang), topped with garlic paste, peanut powder, and pickled vegetables—this "Taiwanese hot dog" concept embodies Taipei people's creative thinking. Tasting this dish at Ningxia Night Market, you'll feel Taipei people's experimental spirit with food.

**Pepper buns** are delicious crystallizations of mainland Chinese culture in Taipei. Crispy pastry wraps pepper-rich pork filling, baked in charcoal ovens until aromatic. The pepper bun stalls at Raohe Night Market always have long lines, proving this dish's status in Taipei people's hearts.

### New Taipei City: Culinary Gathering of Diverse Cultures

**Representative dishes: Tamsui A-gei, Shenkeng stinky tofu, Sanxia golden croissants**

New Taipei City is vast, with each district having its own specialty dishes.

**Tamsui A-gei** is one of New Taipei City's most creative street foods. Fried tofu stuffed with cellophane noodles, sealed and topped with sweet chili sauce—this combination of Japanese ingredients with Taiwanese seasoning reflects Tamsui's multicultural characteristics as a river port town. A-gei stalls on Tamsui Old Street are always bustling during weekends.

**Shenkeng stinky tofu** elevates this classic street food to new heights. Shenkeng's stinky tofu is famous for its deep fermentation and rich textural layers. Various stinky tofu shops on Shenkeng Old Street each have their secret recipes—some emphasizing sourness, others focusing on aroma, forming a unique "Shenkeng stinky tofu culture."

### Taoyuan City: Culinary Heritage of Military Dependents' Village Culture

**Representative dishes: Military village dumplings, Amis stone-grilled meat, Hakka tangyuan**

Taoyuan's diverse ethnic composition has created a rich dining culture.

**Military village dumplings** are Taoyuan's most representative street food. These handmade dumplings passed down from mainland Chinese veterans have thin skins and generous fillings with authentic flavors. Military village dumpling shops are particularly concentrated in the Zhongli Longgang area, each with its own secret recipe.

Due to the large indigenous population, Taoyuan also has many indigenous cuisine restaurants. Amis stone-grilled meat uses traditional indigenous seasoning maqaw (mountain pepper), showcasing flavors different from regular barbecue.

### Hsinchu City and County: Traditional Persistence in a Tech Hub

**Representative dishes: Hsinchu meatballs, Hsinchu rice vermicelli, Zhuchian cakes**

Although Hsinchu is Taiwan's tech hub, it maintains deep traditions in food culture.

**Hsinchu meatballs** are Hsinchu's most famous specialty. Made from fresh pork hand-pounded into balls, they have excellent elasticity and a bouncy texture. Meatball shops around City God Temple have long histories, with Guo Family Meatballs being passed down for four generations, insisting on traditional handmade methods.

**Hsinchu rice vermicelli** is another source of Hsinchu pride. The unique nine-descending-wind climate conditions make Hsinchu's rice vermicelli particularly delicate. Stir-fried rice vermicelli with chives and bean sprouts is simple yet delicious—a childhood memory for many Hsinchu people.

### Yilan County: Rich Flavors Where Mountains Meet the Sea

**Representative dishes: Yilan scallion pancakes, Jiaoxi hot spring eggs, Luodong Night Market fried pork**

Yilan's geographical environment has created a unique food culture.

**Yilan scallion pancakes** use local Sanxing scallions, with rich onion aroma and crispy pancake skin. Scallion pancake stalls at Luodong Night Market and Dongmen Night Market always have long lines, with many tourists traveling to Yilan specifically for this delicacy.

**Jiaoxi hot spring eggs** are specialty snacks from the Jiaoxi hot spring area. Eggs cooked using natural hot spring water have tender whites and semi-runny yolks, served with special dipping sauce—perfect snacks while soaking in hot springs.

## 🌾 Central Region: Perfect Balance of Innovation and Tradition

### Taichung City: Birthplace of Creative Street Food

**Representative dishes: Sun cakes, Dongquan chili sauce, da mian geng**

Taichung is an important birthplace of Taiwan's creative street food—bubble tea was invented here.

**Sun cakes** are Taichung's most famous souvenirs. Paper-thin crispy skin wrapping maltose filling that melts in your mouth creates an unforgettable texture. Sun cake shops on Freedom Road compete fiercely, each with secret recipes, with Tai Yang Tang being one of the oldest establishments.

**Dongquan chili sauce** is Taichung's unique seasoning. This sweet-and-spicy sauce is the soul seasoning for Taichung's stir-fried noodles and da chang bao xiao chang. When Taichung people relocate elsewhere, they must take several bottles of Dongquan chili sauce to create authentic Taichung flavors.

### Changhua County: Kingdom of Meatballs and Common Folk Delicacies

**Representative dishes: Changhua meatballs, braised pork rice, oyster omelets**

Changhua is the birthplace of Taiwan's meatballs, with particularly developed meatball culture here.

**Changhua meatballs** use sweet potato starch to create transparent skins, with fillings typically including pork, bamboo shoots, and shiitake mushrooms. Meatball stalls in Changhua City are numerous, each with its own special sauce. Among them, A-Zhang Meatballs and Authentic Changhua Meatballs are the most popular old shops.

**Braised pork rice** (khòng-bah-pn̄g) is another Changhua specialty. Large chunks of pork belly braised until tender and flavorful, paired with braised eggs and pickled vegetables, served over steaming white rice—the most humble yet satisfying folk food.

### Nantou County: Mountain Produce Food Festival

**Representative dishes: Nantou pasta, Puli rice vermicelli, Sun Moon Lake black tea eggs**

Located in Taiwan's Central Mountain Range, Nantou County's rich mountain produce has created unique food culture.

**Nantou pasta** is local specialty noodles. These handmade egg noodles have a chewy texture, typically served with bone broth and simple side dishes. Pasta shops in the Caotun area are particularly famous, with Pasta King operating for over 50 years.

**Puli rice vermicelli** benefits from Puli's high-quality water sources, creating particularly delicate textures. Rice vermicelli factories in Puli still maintain traditional hand-drying methods, producing high-quality rice vermicelli beloved by consumers.

### Yunlin County: Simple Flavors of an Agricultural County

**Representative dishes: Yunlin old-style sponge cake, Kouhu mullet roe, Douliu meatballs**

Yunlin is Taiwan's agricultural county, with street food here mostly carrying strong rural flavors.

**Yunlin old-style sponge cake** uses traditional recipes without artificial additives, with solid and sweet textures. Places like Huwei and Douliu have famous old-style cake shops that are childhood memories for many Yunlin people.

**Kouhu mullet roe** is a specialty of Yunlin's coastal areas. Kouhu Township has developed aquaculture, producing high-quality mullet roe with delicious taste—a popular gift choice during New Year holidays.

### Chiayi City and County: Hometown of Turkey Rice

**Representative dishes: Chiayi turkey rice, square cookies, white sugar cakes**

Chiayi is most famous for **turkey rice**. This seemingly simple dish is Chiayi people's pride.

Turkey shreds served over white rice, topped with special braised sauce—simple yet delicious. Turkey rice shops in Chiayi City compete fiercely, with Liu Village Chief Turkey Rice and Guo Family Rice Cake Soup Turkey Rice being well-known local establishments. Each shop's turkey processing methods and sauce recipes differ, creating rich taste layers.

**Square cookies** are Chiayi's traditional pastries—crispy outside, soft inside, sweet but not cloying. **White sugar cakes** are common street snacks—fried glutinous rice balls rolled in white sugar powder, sweet and delicious.

## 🏮 Southern Region: Deep Cultural Heritage of the Ancient Capital

### Tainan City: Capital of Food Culture

**Representative dishes: Dan zai noodles, coffin bread, beef soup, milkfish congee**

Tainan is Taiwan's city with the deepest food cultural heritage—every street food here has a story.

**Dan zai noodles** originated from Tainan's fishing culture. Fishermen sold noodles from shoulder poles during off-fishing seasons, hence the name "Du Xiao Yue Dan Zai Noodles." The essence of dan zai noodles lies in the unique meat sauce and shrimp soup. Du Xiao Yue's original shop still maintains traditional preparation methods.

**Coffin bread** is Tainan's unique creative dish. Thick toast is hollowed out and filled with creamy chicken or seafood, shaped like a coffin hence the name. This dish fully demonstrates Tainan people's creativity and humor about food.

**Tainan beef soup** is another source of Tainan pride. Fresh-slaughtered warm beef is quickly blanched in boiling bone broth, creating tender meat and sweet soup. Shops like A-Cun Beef Soup and Six Thousand Beef Soup each have loyal customers.

### Kaohsiung City: Diverse Food Culture of the Port City

**Representative dishes: Gangshan mutton, Cijin seafood, Liuhe Night Market rice tube cakes**

As Taiwan's second-largest city and important port, Kaohsiung has quite diverse food culture.

**Gangshan mutton** is Kaohsiung's most famous specialty food. Mutton hot pot shops in the Gangshan area are concentrated, using unique Chinese medicinal herbs for stewing, effectively removing gamey flavors while keeping mutton fresh and non-fishy. Gangshan Mutton Street has become a must-visit food destination for tourists.

**Cijin seafood** showcases Kaohsiung's fresh seafood as a port city. Seafood restaurants on Cijin Island offer various fresh catches at reasonable prices and fresh flavors. Grilled squid, seafood congee, and fried seafood are must-try dishes.

### Pingtung County: Tropical Island Flavors of Southern Taiwan

**Representative dishes: Wanluan pig knuckles, Donggang seafood, Hengchun mung bean garlic**

Located at Taiwan's southernmost tip, Pingtung's tropical climate and abundant seafood have created unique food culture.

**Wanluan pig knuckles** are Pingtung's most famous specialty. Using unique Chinese medicine packages for long stewing, pig knuckles become tender and flavorful, rich in collagen. Pig knuckle shops line Pig Knuckle Street in Wanluan Township, with Hai Hong Restaurant being the oldest famous establishment.

**Donggang seafood** is renowned for freshness, particularly Donggang's three treasures: bluefin tuna, sakura shrimp, and fish roe. Seafood restaurants around Donggang Fish Market offer the freshest seafood dishes.

## 🏔️ Eastern Region: Pure Mountain and Sea Flavors

### Hualien County: Culinary Treasures of Indigenous Culture

**Representative dishes: Hualien mochi, Gongzheng steamed buns, Yi-xiang flat noodles**

Hualien's geographical environment and indigenous culture have created unique culinary landscapes.

**Hualien mochi** is Hualien's famous souvenir. Made from locally produced purple sweet potatoes, with solid texture and moderate sweetness. Old shops like De Li Tofu and Zeng Ji Mochi both produce Hualien mochi.

**Gongzheng steamed buns** are delicacies that Hualien people have eaten since childhood. Meat buns have thin skins with generous fillings, while soup dumplings have rich broth—signature street food of Hualien's Gongzheng Street.

**Yi-xiang flat noodles** are Hualien's unique noodle dish. Flat noodle skins are paper-thin with fresh fillings, paired with refreshing soup—Hualien people's everyday delicacy.

### Taitung County: Simple Flavors of the Back Mountains

**Representative dishes: Taitung Chishang lunch boxes, custard apples, roselle tea**

Taitung is called Taiwan's "back mountains," where food maintains the most simple flavors.

**Chishang lunch boxes** are Taitung's most famous food. Using high-quality Chishang rice with simple side dishes, they present the original beauty of ingredients. The Chishang Lunch Box Cultural Story Museum preserves this food's history and culture.

**Custard apples** are Taitung's specialty fruit—sweet flesh with creamy texture. Taitung's custard apple production accounts for over 90% of Taiwan's total, with excellent quality as an important agricultural product.

## 🏝️ Offshore Islands: Unique Flavors of Marine Culture

### Penghu County: Seafood Kingdom of the Chrysanthemum Islands

**Representative dishes: Penghu four treasures, cactus ice, salty crackers**

Surrounded by sea, Penghu has particularly developed seafood culture.

**Penghu four treasures** refer to scallops, sea urchins, stone oysters, and purple seaweed—all specialties from Penghu waters. Seafood restaurants in Magong City offer the freshest Penghu seafood.

**Cactus ice** is Penghu's unique summer refresher. Made from wild cactus fruits from Penghu, with refreshing taste and bright colors—a must-try delicacy when visiting Penghu in summer.

### Kinmen County: Special Foods of Battlefield Culture

**Representative dishes: Kinmen tribute candy, sorghum liquor, oyster vermicelli**

Kinmen's unique historical background has created distinctive food culture.

**Kinmen tribute candy** is Kinmen's most famous specialty. Using ingredients like peanuts and sesame seeds, paired with Kinmen sorghum liquor fragrance, creating crispy and sweet textures. Shengzu Tribute Candy and Kinmen Distillery attached stores are good places to buy tribute candy.

**Kinmen sorghum liquor** is famous wine across the Taiwan Strait. Besides direct consumption, it's often used in cooking seasonings like sorghum cakes and sorghum ice cream.

### Matsu Islands: Preserved Mindong Flavors

**Representative dishes: Jiguang cakes, old wine vermicelli, ding-bian-hu**

Matsu preserves complete Mindong (eastern Fujian) food culture.

**Jiguang cakes** are Matsu's traditional snacks, reportedly named to commemorate Qi Jiguang. The cake body is hard and can be stored without spoiling—the main food for fishermen going to sea in early times. Modern Jiguang cakes have richer flavors, both sweet and salty.

**Old wine vermicelli** uses Matsu's specialty old wine (red wine lees wine) for cooking, with rich aroma—Matsu people's home-style delicacy.

## Cultural Significance of the Food Map

These local street foods are not just food—they are cultural carriers from all parts of Taiwan. They record immigrant footprints, witness historical changes, and bear people's memories.

Behind every local street food is a story about this land. It might be wisdom crystallized from early immigrants adapting to new environments, innovative fusion of local ingredients with foreign techniques, or delicious traces left by special historical events.

When we taste these local street foods, we're not just satisfying our taste buds; we're experiencing Taiwan's rich and diverse culture. This is the true value of Taiwan's local street food map—it allows us to deeply understand the cultural characteristics and humanistic customs of various parts of Taiwan through taste.
