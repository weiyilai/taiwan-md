# From Deserts Chang to Anpu — Two Names, One Question

> **30-Second Overview:** Chiao An-pu (焦安溥), performing as "Deserts Chang" (張懸), became the defining voice of Taiwan's indie music scene. She wrote "Bao Bei" at 13. In 2013, she held up a Republic of China flag at a concert in Manchester — China banned her. In 2015, she retired her stage name and vanished for three years. She returned as "Anpu," won Song of the Year at the Golden Melody Awards with a song she'd written at 14, then stunned Taiwan in 2024 by congratulating China on its national day. The same person lost her audience twice, for completely opposite reasons.

## Only Two Tables at Witch House

During those years when Chiao An-pu still went by Deserts Chang, her father Chiao Jen-ho worried about her every day.

Chiao Jen-ho had served as secretary-general of the Straits Exchange Foundation (SEF) — Taiwan's semi-official body for negotiating with China — and was President Lee Teng-hui's chief speechwriter. In 1995, he formulated the "one China, respective interpretations" (一中各表) framework, making him the most important figure at Taiwan's cross-strait negotiating table.[^1] His eldest son, Chiao Yuan-pu, went on to become Taiwan's most renowned classical music critic, interviewing fifty-five of the world's greatest pianists. His youngest daughter, Chiao Tzu-pu, pursued law. Of the three children, only the middle one dropped out of high school, drank, smoked, and never got past an incomplete secondary education.

Deserts Chang worked at Trader Vic's restaurant for eighty New Taiwan dollars an hour. At night, she played at Witch House (女巫店), one of Taipei's legendary indie venues. Chiao Jen-ho didn't dare go listen. He sent his youngest daughter with two classmates to report back. The report made his heart sink: only two tables of customers, one a couple sitting far away. The only people seated in front of the stage were the sister and her two friends.[^2]

"Can you really make a living from music?" he said. "How many self-proclaimed musicians end up playing violin in subway tunnels?"[^2]

Deserts Chang shouted back: "Dad! I'm going to be famous!"

This girl who couldn't afford next month's rent, who wouldn't spend two hundred NT on a taxi home, was willing to slam the table in front of the man who had sat across from China's negotiators — all for music.[^2] Years later, Chiao Jen-ho described their relationship with a metaphor: a kite. He let out enough string for the kite to fly, but never let the wind carry it away.[^2]

> **📝 Curator's Note:** A cross-strait negotiator's daughter chose a stage name that means "suspended" — as in unresolved. Chiao Jen-ho has never attended a single one of his daughter's concerts. But he tells everyone, proudly, that in addition to being the former SEF secretary-general, he is "Deserts Chang's father."[^2]

## The Family War at Thirteen

Chiao An-pu was born on May 30, 1981, in Taipei. Her paternal grandfather, Chiao Tien-kuei, was one of China's first-generation lawyers.[^1] In this family of law and politics, she was what her father called the Lin Daiyu of the household — "No matter how carefully you speak to her, you'll still hurt her." Her sister Tzu-pu was the graceful, easygoing Xue Baochai: "She'd finish her milk and go right to sleep." An-pu, on the other hand, "would take two hours to drink 200 cc of milk."[^2]

At thirteen, she fought with her family, slammed the door, and walked out humming a melody.[^3] That song was "Bao Bei" (Baby) — "My baby, baby, let me give you something sweet, so you can sleep well tonight." It would become the lullaby every parent in Taiwan knows. But it was originally a girl singing to comfort herself.

In high school she began writing poetry, using the pen name "Chang Hsuan" (張懸) — "hsuan" carries the meaning of suspension, something left hanging.[^4] Her reading list ran through Kafka, Mishima Yukio, and later Shen Congwen, Zheng Chouyu, Bei Dao, and T.S. Eliot's _The Waste Land_.[^5] She spent twenty years reading _The Waste Land_, telling _Unitas Literature Monthly_ in 2022: you have to abandon the attempt to decode the metaphors — the real meaning comes from the background and form created by Eliot's massive web of quotations.[^5] She once said: "Books pushed me and made me into a singer."[^5]

At sixteen, she walked into her parents' bedroom and said she was dropping out. Chiao Jen-ho said only: "Okay, I understand." The tears and declarations of purpose she had prepared suddenly had nowhere to go. "No one is going to argue with you anymore," she recalled later. "You can never feel sorry for yourself again."[^2] It was the first time she felt, acutely, that her life was entirely the result of her own decisions.

Her father sent her to a host family in England. She couldn't stand the 8 PM curfew and fled back to Taiwan.[^2] After returning: restaurant shifts, Witch House gigs. Father and daughter communicated through letters. Both still keep the other's correspondence.[^2]

## From Witch House to Golden Melody

In 2003, she took the stage at the Hohaiyan Rock Festival as vocalist of the band Mango Runs, winning both the Most Popular Act and Independent Music awards.[^6] Over the next few years, she became the most frequently performing singer-songwriter in Taipei's live house circuit. By nineteen, she had written over a hundred songs.

Her debut album _My Life Will…_ came out in 2006 on Sony BMG. But the recording had actually been completed in 2001 — she'd signed a deal, then the label restructured and shelved the master tapes for five years. It took producer Lee Shou-chuan hearing her live at Witch House again to convince her to re-sign.[^7] The album received four nominations at the 18th Golden Melody Awards, including Best Mandarin Album and Song of the Year for "Bao Bei."[^7] She didn't win, but every corner of the indie scene knew she had arrived.

Her 2009 album _City_ marked a stylistic turn. She practiced electric guitar obsessively, formed a band called Algae, and shifted from folk to rock and garage.[^8] She was no longer "indie-cute," but her following only grew more devoted.

2012's _Games We Play_ was the real watershed. She wrote nine of the ten tracks and served as co-producer.[^9] The opening song, "Rose-Colored You" (玫瑰色的你), was written for "people who devote their lives to things that truly matter, who can never be bought off by small favors": street activists, independent publishers, everyone pushing forward against the wind.[^10] The song later became the unofficial soundtrack of the Sunflower Movement, and won her Best Lyricist at the 24th Golden Melody Awards.[^9]

"Rose-Colored You" was reportedly censored and removed in China.[^9] A songwriter who wrote songs for protesters had reached the peak of Taiwan's indie world. Then she decided to kill the name "Deserts Chang."

## The Flag in Manchester

November 2, 2013. Manchester, UK. A small concert venue, roughly five hundred people.[^6] Most of the audience were Chinese international students. A few Taiwanese students in the front row brought a Republic of China flag. Deserts Chang took it, held it against her chest, and spoke in English:

> "It's just a flag. It shows where I come from. Why do you have to make it about politics?"[^11]

A Chinese student shouted: "No politics today!"

That sentence ended Deserts Chang's career in China. A boycott campaign on Weibo forced the cancellation of her scheduled Beijing concert.[^12] Back in Taiwan, tens of thousands crowned her a "patriotic singer." She was stunned: "You've got the wrong idea."[^13]

A month later, in an interview with the Taipei Times, she said: "If Taiwan's national identity aspires to higher international visibility, it will inevitably be challenged. I'm not the first person to face this. If I didn't do it, someone else would."[^11]

She had just held up a flag. But across the Taiwan Strait, nothing is ever "just" anything.

## Sunflowers and the Last Tide

After the flag incident, Deserts Chang did not retreat. When the Sunflower Movement erupted in March 2014, she posted repeatedly on Facebook, criticizing the government's forced passage of a cross-strait trade agreement and urging people "not to give up your right to engage in civic issues."[^14] She had long spoken out for the anti-nuclear movement, water rights disputes, and marriage equality.[^4] "Rose-Colored You" became the soundtrack of street protests — but she never wanted to be called a "political singer."

"To Ebb" (潮水箴言) was a concert series she had been building since 2010, growing from thousand-seat venues like Legacy to the ten-thousand-seat Nangang Exhibition Center. She served as both producer and art director, designing each show as a contemporary art exhibition rather than a commercial concert: literary texts as foundation, cinematic storytelling techniques, every song with its own visual world.[^15]

In January 2015, the final "To Ebb" show closed at the Kaohsiung Arena.[^16] After the last note, she announced: the name Deserts Chang ends here.

Her reason for returning to her birth name was a single sentence: "Anpu is the name my parents gave me. I simply want them — who have a habit of clipping newspaper articles — to see these two characters when they collect clippings about me."[^17]

Then she disappeared.

## Three Years of Silence, and a Cat

The trigger for the hiatus was a cat. During the promotional tour for _Games We Play_, the cat she'd had since she was a teenager was diagnosed with a tumor. Caught between professional obligations and the animal's final days, the anxiety became unbearable. She chose to stop.[^18]

During those three years, she read, practiced calligraphy, wrote poetry, strengthened her instrumental skills, and studied stage production.[^19] She accompanied the cat through its last days. She once wrote on Facebook: the attention she received had never truly belonged to her.

This silence was essential. Over twelve years, Deserts Chang had calcified into a symbol: indie music, social activism, national identity. The symbol was too heavy. She needed to put it down to find out what was left underneath.

## Refine: A Statement in 3,200 LED Panels

In May 2018, she returned as "Anpu" with a concert called "Refine" (煉雲) at Taipei Arena. Twenty thousand tickets sold out instantly.[^20]

The production cost 36 million NT. The main stage was assembled from over 3,200 irregularly shaped LED panels forming cloud formations — the stage alone ate through 15 million NT and took six months to design.[^20] From over two hundred candidate songs, she chose twenty-two covers: all works by Taiwanese musicians who had influenced her, spanning thirty years of underground and indie history. Chao Yi-hao, Chen Shan-ni, Huang Hsiao-chen, Lin Chung. For the twenty thousand people in the audience, most of these were "new songs" they had never heard.

Music critic Ma Shih-fang wrote: "A magnificent dream." He described the stage built like stacked blocks, creating a sensory experience that was precise, sweeping, breathtaking — yet never extravagant.[^20]

She sang exactly one of her own songs — "Bao Bei." Reportedly, she cried after finishing it.

"Refine" was her word for what this show meant: distilling nothingness from existence, endlessly simplifying the complex.[^20] This was not a comeback concert. It was a declaration: I am back, but I am not the person you remember.

## 9522: A Letter to Girls Growing Up

On January 18, 2022, Anpu announced her divorce during a concert, saying the paperwork had been finalized that afternoon. She and animation director Su Po-wei had been married two and a half years and had a son.[^21] She publicly called her ex-husband "the bravest man in the Chinese-speaking world" for daring to marry her.[^21] She said she planned to see a therapist after the Lunar New Year, openly acknowledging she needed professional support.[^21]

Nine months later, the album _9522_ was released. Sixteen tracks, nearly all written between the ages of fourteen and seventeen. The cryptic title encoded the span from 1995 to 2022 — twenty-seven years.[^22] A forty-year-old woman going back to sing songs a teenager had written, framed as "a letter of blessing to girls and women who are growing up."[^22]

"A Flash and How It Lasts" (最好的時光) was the lead single, its melody written at fourteen. She had once submitted it as a car commercial jingle and been rejected. Years later, actress Chang Chun-ning heard the demo and was so moved she encouraged Anpu to finish it. Wu Qing-feng was the first person to hear the completed version: "This moment makes this life worth it." The two stayed up until dawn listening to each other's albums.[^23]

In 2023, "A Flash and How It Lasts" won Song of the Year at the 34th Golden Melody Awards.[^24] The jury's comment: "A work that transcends era and time — a song you can listen to forever." On that night, the name on the trophy was not Deserts Chang. It was Anpu.

> **💡 Did you know?** When Chiao Yuan-pu interviews world-class pianists, he brings Deserts Chang's CDs as gifts. He introduces her as: "The musician in our family."[^25]

## The Handwritten Letter on October First

At 10:01 PM on October 1, 2024, Anpu's management company posted a handwritten note on Weibo: "Wishing the 75th anniversary of the founding of New China — may the people be harmonious, and peace extend ten thousand miles." Signed: Anpu. The timing was precisely calibrated to China's National Day.[^26]

The person who had been banned from China for holding a Republic of China flag eleven years earlier was now wishing the People's Republic of China a happy birthday.

Taiwanese fans' reaction was not anger. It was heartbreak. The person who wrote "Rose-Colored You," who spoke up during the Sunflower Movement, who said "It's just a flag" in Manchester — how could she write that letter? Her close friend He Hsin-sui tried to soften the blow and was attacked for it.[^27] One writer analyzed her recent trajectory and called it "a long-planned pledge of allegiance."[^28] Political scientist Huang Chao-nien pointed out that this was part of China's "Three Mediums, One Young" (三中一青) united front strategy: pressuring Taiwanese entertainers into political statements to influence young people's sense of identity.[^29]

Eleven days later, October 12. The Takao Rock Festival in Kaohsiung. Before Anpu took the stage, the crowd was already waving Taiwanese independence whale flags and Yushan flags. Someone shouted: "Chiao An-pu — why?"

She did not answer directly. She said something else:[^30]

> "At my shows, you are always free and safe. The world is big, and I can't guarantee that every place is the same. But at least where I perform, you can be yourself."

Then she bowed, deeply, to the people holding those flags.

Fire EX. frontman Yang Da-zheng spent seven minutes addressing the issue during his own set at Takao Rock. He told the audience: if you're disappointed, you can walk away — but don't go witch-hunting.[^30]

## You Can Be Yourself

Eleven years ago in Manchester, she said, "It's just a flag." Eleven years later at Takao Rock, she said, "You can be yourself." Both sentences point to the same thing: a refusal to define your freedom for you, and a refusal to let you define hers.

At forty-three, Anpu says she is living in "the most honest phase of my life," shedding the unanswerable questions of her youth and learning to cherish things that never needed answers in the first place.[^31] She says she is not an authority, just "someone who accompanies others through a change of mind."[^31] She looks forward to aging: "Society demands that women always look young. That exhausts me. The freedom that comes after you're liberated from hormones and other people's gazes — that is truly happy aging."[^31]

You can say she changed. You can also say she has been doing the same thing all along — living in the gray zone where no flag is big enough and no name can contain who she is.

In a 2019 interview, Chiao Jen-ho said that the most precious gift Deserts Chang ever gave him was showing him that the world is diverse, and that life is a multiple-choice question — not a single-choice one.[^2]

Perhaps the daughter learned the same thing from her father. It's just that the answer she chose is the one neither side wants to hear.
