# Taiwan Coastal Landforms and Marine Landscapes

> **30-Second Overview:** Along Taiwan's 1,600-kilometer coastline, Qingshui Cliffs plunge 800 meters straight into the Pacific, Yehliu's Queen's Head thins by 0.2cm annually, and Kenting's coral reefs struggle under rising sea level threats. These landscapes aren't just beautiful—they're witnesses to 9 million years of tectonic plate collisions.

Standing on the Qingshui Cliffs observation platform along the Suhua Highway, there's an 800-meter vertical drop beneath your feet. Pacific waves crash against the cliff base, creating white foam that looks ant-sized. This massive marble wall records 9 million years of violent collisions between the Philippine Sea Plate and the Eurasian Plate.

At Taiwan's northern tip in Yehliu, tourists eagerly photograph the Queen's Head, few knowing she faces a "decapitation crisis"—after years of wave erosion, her neck thins by 0.2cm annually. Geologists predict this elegant queen may collapse within decades.

This characterizes Taiwan's coastal landforms: magnificent yet fragile, ancient yet young, constantly changing on geological timescales while facing unprecedented threats on human timescales.

## Four Coasts, Four Destinies

Taiwan's coastline stretches approximately 1,600 kilometers. Based on geological structure and morphological features, it divides into four major types, each with its own "geological personality."

### North Coast: Art Gallery of Weathering and Marine Erosion

The north coast, from Tamsui River mouth to Sandiaojiao Cape, typifies rocky coastlines. Yehliu Geopark is most famous here, though it's merely one example of north coast landforms.

Yehliu's rock layers belong to the Miocene Taliao Formation (formed about 23 million years ago), mainly composed of thick sandstone layers. When seawater constantly erodes these sandstone layers, combined with strong waves from northeastern monsoons, various unique marine erosion landforms are carved.

The Queen's Head exemplifies "mushroom rocks." Her formation process is straightforward: hard sandstone layers protect softer rock layers beneath, but as waves continuously erode the base, the supporting "neck" becomes increasingly thin. Geologists say the Queen's Head has entered the final stage of mushroom rock evolution—neck breaking is just a matter of time.

But this "beauty's demise" embodies the essence of geological processes. Near the Queen's Head, many mushroom rocks are still forming—when the Queen's Head falls, new stone sculptures are already preparing to debut.

### East Coast: Direct Evidence of Plate Compression

The east coast presents Taiwan's most dramatic landscape. Qingshui Cliffs average over 800 meters in height, with the highest points approaching 1,000 meters, plunging almost vertically into the Pacific.

This cliff formation dates back about 9 million years when the Philippine Sea Plate began head-on collision with the Eurasian Plate. Immense pressure pushed originally submarine sedimentary rocks upward and metamorphosed them into marble, gneiss, and other metamorphic rocks. Later fault activity created vertical cliff faces, and Pacific wave erosion produced today's Qingshui Cliffs.

Notably, Qingshui Cliffs aren't static. GPS monitoring shows this area still rises several centimeters annually. In other words, plate collision continues, and Qingshui Cliffs are still "growing taller."

The east coast also features unique landforms: marine terraces. From Hualien to Taitung coast, layered step-like terrain resembles giant staircases distributed along mountain bases. These marine terraces record past sea level changes and crustal uplift history, providing important clues for studying Quaternary geological changes.

### West Coast: Sand Kingdom and Intertidal Zones

The west coast, extending from Tamsui River mouth to Fenggang in Pingtung, is Taiwan's longest coastal segment and most "gentle" coast. Here you won't find east coast precipices or north coast strange rocks, but broad sandy beaches, mudflats, and lagoons.

This difference stems from geological structure. The west is a continental shelf extension of Taiwan Strait, with gentle submarine slopes. Combined with massive sediment loads from Zhuoshui River, Zengwen River, and other major rivers, this formed vast alluvial fans and deltas.

But the west coast faces serious coastal erosion problems. Changhua, Yunlin, and Chiayi coasts retreat 10-15 meters annually, with some areas experiencing worse erosion. Causes are complex: upstream sand barriers reduce sediment supply; groundwater over-pumping causes land subsidence; sea level rise aggravates flooding risks. Most seriously, artificial coastal facilities (like jetties and tetrapods) alter natural longshore sediment transport, often protecting one area while damaging another.

### South Coast: Last Fortress of Living Coral Reefs

The south coast around Kenting is Taiwan's only tropical coral reef coast. Year-round water temperatures of 23-28°C perfectly suit reef-building coral growth.

Hengchun Peninsula has two types of coral reefs: modern coral reefs and uplifted coral reefs. Modern coral reefs exist near sea level, built by living coral polyps; uplifted coral reefs are former reefs elevated above sea level through crustal uplift, forming today's high-level coral reef platforms.

But Taiwan's coral reefs face multiple threats. Rising seawater temperatures cause coral bleaching—summer 2023's marine heat waves killed massive coral populations in Kenting waters. Ocean acidification makes it difficult for corals to form calcium carbonate skeletons. Land pollution and tourism pressure also damage coral reefs.

## The Real Threat of Sea Level Rise

The greatest long-term threat to Taiwan's coasts is sea level rise. According to Central Weather Administration data, sea levels in Taiwan waters rise at 3.4mm annually, exceeding the global average.

This seemingly minor change has major impacts. For west coast lowlands, a few centimeters of sea level rise can cause seawater intrusion and saltwater infiltration into farmland. For coral reefs, while sea level rise provides more growing space, accompanying temperature increases and acidification effects prove lethal.

The government has begun promoting coastal management legislation, hoping to implement "coastal retreat" concepts—gradually removing development from the most vulnerable coastal areas and returning them to natural states. But this involves massive relocation costs and complex social issues, making implementation challenging.

## More Than Scenery—Taiwan's Life Story

Taiwan's coastal landforms aren't just beautiful scenery but witnesses to this island's life story.

Qingshui Cliffs tell us Taiwan remains a "young" island with ongoing intense plate collisions. Yehliu's strange rocks tell us geological processes never cease—scenery disappears, but new beauty emerges. West coast erosion tells us human activities have profoundly altered natural balance. South coast coral reefs tell us climate change isn't a future threat but an ongoing crisis.

Each coastal landform tells stories—about time, about forces, about change, and about how we coexist with nature. When we stand seaside admiring these landscapes, we might consider: we're seeing not just rocks and seawater, but artistic creations jointly crafted by 9 million years of internal Earth forces and external processes.

And this creative process continues still.
