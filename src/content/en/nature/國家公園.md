# National Parks

> **30-second overview:** Taiwan is only 36,197 square kilometers, yet houses 9 national parks—the highest density in the world.
> In this most crowded conservation system, Taiwan black bear populations doubled in 20 years, black-faced spoonbills grew from 288 to 6,988 globally,
> and Formosan landlocked salmon exploded from 200 endangered fish to 16,020. Small island, big conservation.

How do you squeeze 9 national parks into an island smaller than Switzerland?

In 1984, when Kenting National Park became Taiwan's first national park, no one imagined it would mark the beginning of a density experiment. Forty-two years later, Taiwan hosts 9 national parks and 1 national natural park across 36,197 square kilometers, protecting 750,000 hectares—a density that is likely world-record territory.

Even more remarkable: this most crowded conservation system created the globe's most successful species recovery miracles.

| 288 → 6,988 | 200 → 16,020 | Population Doubled |
|---|---|---|
| Black-faced spoonbills globally (1989→2024) | Formosan landlocked salmon (1990s→2025) | Taiwan black bears (20 years) |

## The Density Experiment: How Taiwan Became a National Park Island

**42 years of national park expansion:**

1. **1984-1986** — The founding trinity: Kenting, Yushan, Yangmingshan
2. **1987-1995** — Mountains and seas: Taroko, Shei-Pa
3. **1995-2014** — Diversification: Kinmen (battlefield heritage), Dongsha (pure marine), Taijiang (wetlands), South Penghu Marine (basalt)
4. **2011** — Urban oasis: Shoushan National Natural Park (within Kaohsiung city)

Each step challenged traditional national park definitions. Kinmen focuses on battlefield heritage, Dongsha is 99.9% marine area, Taijiang blends cultural and wetland conservation, while South Penghu Marine consists of uninhabited sea paradise.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Taiwan's national park evolution follows a unique path: from "wilderness protection"
> (American model) to "cultural-natural integration" (European concept), finally developing
> a distinct "three-dimensional land-sea-air protection network."

This density is almost unprecedented internationally. For comparison: Yellowstone National Park alone covers 8,991 square kilometers—Taiwan's entire territory is only 4 times larger, yet accommodates 9 national parks.

## Taiwan's Species Recovery Miracle

### Black-faced Spoonbills: From Near Extinction to 1/6 of Global Population in Taiwan

In 1989, Hong Kong Bird Watching Society's first global count revealed a shocking number: 288 black-faced spoonbills. This figure stunned the global conservation community—a species stood so close to extinction.

Thirty-five years later, the 2024 global census shows 6,988 black-faced spoonbills, with over 1,000 gathering annually in Taijiang National Park, making it the world's most important wintering habitat.

**This isn't natural recovery—it's precision conservation engineering.** Taijiang National Park doesn't just protect over 40,000 hectares of wetlands; it collaborates with fishermen and salt workers, allowing traditional industries to coexist with ecological protection. Each autumn and winter, when the first black-faced spoonbills land at Sicao wetlands, Tainan locals know: the international-class wetland brand still shines.

### Formosan Landlocked Salmon: From 200 Fish to 16,020—A Resurrection Story

In the 1990s, Formosan landlocked salmon populations hit rock bottom with only about 200 individuals remaining in the wild, facing extinction. This Ice Age relic "national treasure fish" can only survive in streams above 1,500 meters elevation with water temperatures below 17°C—climate change and habitat destruction left them nowhere to retreat.

Shei-Pa National Park spent 30 years multiplying this number to 16,020.

> **💡 Did You Know**
> In September 2025, Shei-Pa Management Office first used helicopter
> lifting to release over 1,000 salmon fry into upstream Syjielan Creek.
> This was Taiwan's first "airdrop restoration," allowing fish to
> quickly reach optimal habitat.

The key to recovery wasn't technology—it was patience. Wuling Farm recovered 8.1 hectares of abandoned farmland, Chichiawan Creek's 20-year reforestation program, cross-watershed release strategies—each step required years of planning. Today, Syjielan Creek, Chichiawan Creek, and Hehuan Creek all host stable populations, removing Formosan landlocked salmon from frequent endangered species lists.

### Taiwan Black Bears: From Endangered to "No Longer at Risk of Extinction"

In April 2025, the Forestry and Nature Conservation Agency publicly stated for the first time: Taiwan black bears have "no risk of extinction," with population numbers "absolutely multiple times" higher than 20 years ago.

Behind this statement lies 40 years of accumulated conservation effort. High-altitude national parks like Yushan, Taroko, and Shei-Pa provided core habitat for Taiwan black bears. Recent years show bears expanding into areas below 1,200 meters elevation, indicating stable populations beginning natural expansion.

> **⚠️ Success's Price**
> Taiwan black bear recovery success brings new challenges. In 2025,
> a 120-kilogram bear in Hualien's Zhuoxi tribe repeatedly attacked
> livestock and pets, ultimately requiring lethal removal. Human-bear
> conflict will be the next phase's conservation focus.

## World-Class High-Density Conservation Model

Taiwan's national park system succeeds not through size but **diversity and management precision**.

From 3,952-meter Yushan Main Peak to below-sea-level Dongsha Atoll; from volcanic geology of Yangmingshan to basalt columnar joints of South Penghu Marine; from Kinmen's battlefield heritage to Shoushan's urban forest—9 national parks cover all major ecosystem and geological types in Taiwan.

**This is a three-dimensional protection network:**
- **Alpine zone**: Yushan, Shei-Pa, Taroko protecting mountain ecosystems and large mammals
- **Volcanic zone**: Yangmingshan guarding Datun volcanic group and temperate vegetation
- **Coastal zone**: Kenting and South Penghu Marine protecting coral reef ecosystems
- **Wetland zone**: Taijiang maintaining international wetlands and migratory bird routes
- **Offshore zone**: Kinmen and Dongsha handling battlefield heritage and marine conservation respectively

### Urban National Park Experiment

In 2011, Shoushan National Natural Park was established—Taiwan's first national-level protection area within a metropolitan region. In Kaohsiung's southwestern corner, 1,131 hectares of coral limestone terrain protect Taiwan's endemic Formosan rock macaques and the island's largest mountain ebony forest.

**This experiment challenged a traditional concept: must national parks be far from cities?** Shoushan proved that even at urban fringes, strict protection management can maintain ecological integrity.

> **✦** "Big conservation on a small island—Taiwan proves density isn't conservation's enemy; precision is."

## Challenges and Controversies: Growing Pains

### Recreation Pressure: National Parks Are Not Amusement Parks

Taiwan's national parks face their biggest controversy from a fundamental misunderstanding: many people treat "national parks" as "parks built by the nation."

Professor Wang Zhenling from Feng Chia University's Land Management Department observed in 2015 (still applicable): "Most people, even government officials, have literal misunderstandings of 'national parks.'" Massive tourist influxes to Kenting and Taroko demand railings, warning signs, even proposals for power cables atop Yushan and hotels at Yangmingshan Maocao.

**National Park Law Article 1 clearly states: protect nationally representative natural scenery, wildlife, and historical sites, "and provide public recreation and research."** "Recreation" is secondary, limited to recreation and general control zones, and cannot conflict with conservation purposes.

### Climate Change: Survival Crisis for Alpine Species

Seventy percent of Taiwan's national park area lies above 1,000 meters elevation, making alpine ecosystems particularly vulnerable to climate change. A 1°C temperature rise forces alpine plant distribution 150-200 meters upward, but mountaintops have limited space—nowhere left to retreat.

Formosan landlocked salmon's successful recovery is partly a race against time. When stream temperatures exceed 17°C, these national treasure fish cannot survive. Climate change turns restoration work into a "race between temperature and conservation speed."

### Indigenous Rights: Balancing Conservation and Tradition

Seventy-five percent of Taiwan's national parks overlap with indigenous traditional territories. Balancing conservation needs with indigenous rights remains a sensitive issue. Bunun people at Yushan, Atayal at Shei-Pa, Truku at Taroko—they were these lands' earliest guardians, now potentially restricted in traditional lifestyles by conservation regulations.

Recent years see national park management offices collaborating with tribes, promoting "community conservation" models—hiring local residents as conservation volunteers in Kinmen, cooperating with fishermen for wetland ecology in Taijiang. This approach shifts conservation work from "external control" to "local participation."

## Next 40 Years: The Marine National Park Era?

If the past 40 years established the world's highest-density national park system, the next 40 will focus on **oceans**.

Dongsha Atoll National Park is 99.5% marine area, while South Penghu Marine National Park's marine area comprises 98%. This trend reflects global conservation thinking's shift: from "land-first" to "land-sea balance."

**Taiwan's surrounding waters hold massive conservation potential:** The Kuroshio Current brings rich marine biodiversity, coral coverage exceeds 60% in some areas, and seagrass bed ecosystems provide important carbon sequestration. But marine conservation complexity far exceeds terrestrial—maritime jurisdiction, fishing rights, international regulations make each aspect hundred times more difficult than establishing land protected areas.

> **📊 Future Outlook Data**
> According to National Park Service planning, Taiwan's marine protected
> area target is 10% of the exclusive economic zone by 2030—about
> 200,000 square kilometers. This equals 6 times Taiwan proper's area.

## Small Island's Big Vision

Returning to the initial question: why does an island smaller than Switzerland need 9 national parks?

The answer might not be about Taiwan's smallness, but its complexity. Within 36,197 square kilometers, this island contains all climate zones from tropical to frigid, complete plate collision geological history, cultural stratification from Austronesian to Han peoples, plus crucial migratory bird route nodes.

**Taiwan's national park system significance lies not in scale but completeness.** It protects not just 9 points, but an island's genetic code.

Forty-two years ago, when Kenting National Park was established, Taiwan was still under martial law. Today, on this world's highest-density national park island, black-faced spoonbill numbers increased 24-fold, Formosan landlocked salmon populations multiplied 80-fold, and Taiwan black bears are no longer endangered.

This might be the world's most successful high-density conservation experiment: proving that even in the most constrained spaces, refined conservation work can still create miracles.
