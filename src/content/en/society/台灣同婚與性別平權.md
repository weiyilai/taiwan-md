# Marriage Equality and Gender Rights in Taiwan

> **30-second overview:** In 1986, Chi Chia-wei was imprisoned for 5 months under martial law for coming out publicly. On May 17, 2019, the same person's constitutional petition made Taiwan the first country in Asia to legalize same-sex marriage. This isn't just a story about laws — it's about 30 years of refusing to give up, about how someone society called a "monster" eventually made society redefine love itself.

---

## A Marriage Certificate 33 Years in the Making

May 24, 2019. Xinyi District Household Registration Office, Taipei.

At 6 AM, hundreds of same-sex couples queued outside the office. When the first same-sex marriage certificate was handed over, the waiting crowd erupted in cheers and tears. Some embraced and wept, others threw rainbow flags skyward, while some simply stared quietly at that thin piece of paper in their hands — bearing two words they'd waited a lifetime to see: "spouse."

61-year-old Chi Chia-wei wasn't there. Not because he didn't have a partner, but because for him, this victory came too late. From his first application for same-sex marriage registration in 1986 to the law's passage in 2019, he had spent 33 years fighting.

**During these 33 years, he was imprisoned, socially ostracized, mocked by media, and cursed at by passersby while standing alone on streets with a rainbow flag.** But each time he was rejected, he applied again. Each time he lost a case, he appealed again.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Taiwan's marriage equality victory didn't happen because society suddenly became enlightened — it happened because someone refused to be forgotten by their time.

## "Major Harm" Under Martial Law

### 1986: A Press Conference That Changed Everything

February 28, 1986. Taiwan was still under martial law.

On this day, Chi Chia-wei held an international press conference at McDonald's, coming out publicly as Taiwan's — and possibly Asia's — first openly gay person. He invited international media including Reuters to cover AIDS prevention advocacy. He was 28 years old.

**Five months later, he was arrested by the government on charges of "major harm."**

"The person who arrested me said: 'Mr. Chi, you're too capable, so we must make you disappear from the free world and imprison you for five years,'" Chi later recalled. But this law student used his knowledge of criminology to successfully gain freedom after 5 months.

After his release, he made an even more audacious decision: applying for same-sex marriage registration.

### The First Application, The First Rejection

In 1986, Chi Chia-wei petitioned Taipei District Court to register marriage with his boyfriend.

Rejection was expected. But Chi wasn't seeking immediate success — he was establishing a **legal precedent**. To transform something from non-existent to existent. From undiscussed to unavoidable discussion.

> **💡 Did You Know?**
> Chi Chia-wei committed to fighting for LGBTQ+ rights from age 17. In 1975, his English teacher taught the word "homosexual." He researched it and discovered that the World Psychiatric Association had removed homosexuality from its list of mental disorders in 1974, confirming his orientation was normal.

## One Man's War

### Society's Cold Stares and Curses

From the late 1980s to early 1990s, LGBTQ+ people in Taiwan faced severe stigma. Chi participated in social movements with his openly gay identity, often facing verbal abuse and ridicule from strangers.

He once dressed as Jesus, carrying a cross on streets to promote AIDS awareness; wore 300 condoms, dressed as Cleopatra at train stations distributing condoms; fundraised for AIDS patients while people avoided him like a plague.

**Senior Taiwan journalist Yang Suo recalled: "Whenever I think of Chi Chia-wei, my mind pictures him in the late 1980s, carrying a fundraising box... Most passersby avoided him from afar, treating him as a plague. Society and media saw him as a 'troublemaker.'"**

But Chi never felt lonely. His strategy wasn't organizing groups or gathering crowds, but **single-handedly challenging every aspect of the system**.

### Thirty Years of Legal Marathon

From 1986 to 2019, Chi exhausted every legal avenue:

- **Administrative Appeals**: Applications, petitions, appeals to all government levels
- **Judicial Litigation**: Civil lawsuits, administrative lawsuits, objections, protests
- **Legislative Petitions**: Repeated proposals to the Legislative Yuan
- **Final Weapon**: 2015 constitutional interpretation petition

Each defeat paved the way for the next victory. **Chi wasn't waiting for society to change — he was creating conditions for societal change.**

---

## Constitutional Interpretation 748: A Historic Judgment

### May 24, 2017: A Turning Point in Asian Constitutional History

On May 24, 2017, Taiwan's Constitutional Court issued Interpretation No. 748, declaring that the Civil Code's failure to protect same-sex marriage was unconstitutional. Taiwan became **the first place in Asia where the highest judicial body recognized same-sex marriage rights as constitutionally protected**.

> **✦** "The Civil Code's failure to allow same-sex couples to form permanent unions for shared life with intimacy and exclusivity violates Article 22's protection of marriage freedom and Article 7's protection of equal rights."

The Constitutional Court gave the legislature two years to amend laws. If legislation wasn't completed by May 24, 2019, same-sex couples could directly register under civil marriage provisions.

### Three Key Legal Foundations

**Marriage Freedom**: A constitutional basic right that shouldn't be restricted based on sexual orientation
**Equal Protection**: Differential treatment based on sexual orientation requires strict scrutiny; current laws constitute unreasonable discrimination
**Personality Rights**: Same-sex couples' intimate relationships are closely related to personality development

This wasn't just a legal victory, but a **revolutionary transformation of human rights concepts**.

---

## 2018 Referendum: Society's Tug-of-War

### The Forces of Reaction

After the constitutional ruling, opposition groups launched "Pro-Family Referendums." On November 24, 2018, same-sex marriage referendums became Taiwan's most intense civic mobilization.

**Referendum results dealt a blow to the marriage equality movement:**
- "Civil marriage should be limited to one man and one woman": 7.65 million yes votes
- "Protect same-sex couples with special law": 6.49 million yes votes  
- Pro-civil code amendment referendum: Only 3.04 million yes votes

### Tears on Referendum Night

On the evening of November 24, 2018, many LGBTQ+ community members and supporters wept at vote-counting venues. Some described it as the pain of "having their existence denied by referendum."

But this setback also prompted important social reflection: **Should fundamental human rights be decided by majority vote?** The extensive use of fear appeals and misinformation during the referendum process also made society recognize the importance of information quality for democratic discussion.

> **⚠️ Controversial Viewpoint**
> Legal scholars generally believe human rights shouldn't be subject to majority rule, but opponents maintain the legitimacy of democratic procedures. This tension continues in Taiwanese society today.

## May 17, 2019: Historic 66 Votes

### International Day Against Homophobia

May 17, 2019 — "International Day Against Homophobia" — the Legislative Yuan passed the "Enforcement Act for Judicial Yuan Constitutional Interpretation No. 748" in its third reading.

**66 votes in favor, 27 against.**

The moment the bill passed, tens of thousands of supporters gathered outside the Legislative Yuan erupted in deafening cheers. In the rain on Ketagalan Boulevard, rainbow flags fluttered as people embraced in tears.

### Compromise But Historic Legislation

This law was a product of political compromise — neither amending the Civil Code nor creating a completely independent special law, but an "enforcement act."

**Legal Content:**
- ✅ **Marriage Rights**: Same-sex couples can legally marry
- ✅ **Property Protection**: Subject to Civil Code marital property provisions
- ✅ **Medical Decisions**: Spousal medical proxy rights
- ✅ **Inheritance Rights**: Full inheritance protection
- ⚠️ **Adoption Limits**: Initially only stepchild adoption allowed
- ⚠️ **Cross-border Limits**: Only with citizens from countries recognizing same-sex marriage

Despite limitations, Taiwan became **the first country in Asia to legally protect same-sex marriage**.

## After Marriage Equality: Evolving Rights

### Gradual Legal Improvements

**January 2023**: Ministry of Interior relaxed cross-border same-sex marriage restrictions, allowing Taiwanese citizens to marry foreigners from most countries not recognizing same-sex marriage

**May 2023**: Legislative Yuan passed amendments allowing same-sex spouses to jointly adopt unrelated children

### Marriage Registration Data

Since opening on May 24, 2019:
- **Total Registrations**: Over 10,000 same-sex couples have completed marriage registration
- **Gender Ratio**: Female couples roughly twice the number of male couples
- **Geographic Distribution**: Not limited to metropolitan areas; rural areas also have registered cases

> **📊 Data Source**
> Ministry of Interior household registration statistics, 2019-2022 cumulative data. Recent registration numbers have stabilized, reflecting gradual satisfaction of demand.

## Taipei Pride: Asia's Rainbow Capital

### From 1,000 to 100,000 Participants

**2003**: Taiwan LGBT Pride founded with ~1,000 participants
**2014**: Over 65,000 participants, becoming Asia's largest
**Recent years**: Consistently over 100,000 participants

Every last Saturday of October, central Taipei is occupied by rainbow flags. Participants include LGBTQ+ communities, straight allies, corporate contingents, foreign diplomats, and international tourists, forming Asia's largest gender equality carnival.

### Local Pride Events Across Taiwan

Beyond Taipei, Kaohsiung (since 2011), Taichung, Tainan, and Hualien also hold Pride events. The development of local Pride events shows that gender issues have expanded from metropolitan topics to concerns throughout Taiwan.

**Taiwan LGBT Pride attracts large numbers of Southeast Asian and East Asian LGBTQ+ people, making Taiwan known as "Asia's LGBTQ+-friendly capital."**

## International Significance: Asia's Lighthouse

### Demonstration Effect

**January 23, 2025**: Thailand's marriage equality law took effect, making it the second Asian country to legalize same-sex marriage. Thailand's legislative process heavily referenced Taiwan's experience.

**Japan, South Korea, Vietnam, Philippines** and other countries' LGBTQ+ movements all draw strategies and courage from Taiwan's case. Taiwan proved that: **Asian societies can maintain cultural traditions while building progressive human rights systems.**

### Soft Power Expression

Marriage equality legislation strengthened Taiwan's international image as "Asia's human rights lighthouse." Facing diplomatic challenges, gender equality became an important field for Taiwan to demonstrate its values.

**International human rights organizations have praised Taiwan as "a model of democracy and human rights in Asia," bringing positive impacts to Taiwan's international standing.**

---

## The Unfinished Revolution

### Issues Still to Address

**Transgender Rights**: Gender marker changes on ID cards still require surgery, raising human rights concerns

**Anti-Discrimination Law**: Taiwan lacks comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation; workplace and educational protections remain insufficient  

**Rural LGBTQ+**: LGBTQ+ people outside metropolitan areas still face challenges in resources and acceptance

**Elder LGBTQ+ Care**: LGBTQ+-friendly long-term care systems need development

### Ongoing Social Dialogue

After marriage equality legislation, Taiwan's discussions on gender issues have become more mature. **Progress from "can we marry" to "how do we live together," from legal equality to social inclusion.**

**Gender-equal education, workplace diversity and inclusion, friendly healthcare environments** — these are the next battlegrounds for Taiwan's gender equality.

---

## Why This Matters

The significance of Taiwan's marriage equality legislation extends far beyond marriage itself:

**Test of Democratic Quality**: How a society treats minorities is an important indicator of its democratic maturity

**Redefining Asian Values**: Taiwan proved that respecting tradition and protecting human rights can coexist

**Triumph of Rule of Law**: From individual applications to constitutional interpretation, demonstrating the legal system's power to protect the vulnerable

**Symbol of Social Progress**: From "crime" under martial law to "right" in the democratic era, reflecting civilizational progress of the entire society

> **✦** "This isn't just a change in law, but progress in civilization. Every couple who can now legally marry because of this represents the protection of human dignity."

---

From Chi Chia-wei's first application under martial law in 1986 to the Legislative Yuan's historic vote in 2019; from one person's lonely war to tens of thousands in rainbow parades — Taiwan's journey took 33 years.

This story tells us: **Change doesn't happen overnight, but as long as someone is willing to stand up, as long as the rule of law can function, equality and dignity will eventually arrive.**

Taiwan's experience also declares to the world: in this divided world, love is the one thing that least needs to be categorized.
