# Taiwan New Media Art: When Code Meets Poetry

When Huang Hsin-chien's VR collaboration with American avant-garde artist Laurie Anderson, "La Camera Insabbiata" (Chalkroom), won the inaugural VR Best Experience Award at the 2017 Venice Film Festival, few understood the key insight behind this breakthrough: **Taiwan new media art's core strength isn't tech wizardry, but the ability to write poetry with code.**

How unique is this phenomenon? While Western artists treat technology as a tool, Taiwanese artists treat it as a language. While Japanese and Korean new media art moves toward commercialized sophistication, Taiwan's creators ponder "how to give machines souls."

Since the Taipei Digital Art Festival launched in 2006, Taiwan new media art has undergone a complete transformation from periphery to mainstream, from imitation to innovation. Today, whether at Venice Biennale or Art Basel Miami, Taiwanese artists' works distinguish themselves through a unique "warm technology" aesthetic, providing human warmth to the cold digital age.

## 30-Second Overview

**Why is Taiwan New Media Art Important?**

Taiwan new media art represents a unique branch in global tech art development: it's neither pure technical exhibition nor digitization of traditional art, but the creation of a new language of "poetic technology." From Huang Hsin-chien's VR meditation spaces to LuxuryLogico's mechanical gardens, from C-LAB's laboratories to generative art on fxHash platforms, Taiwanese artists prove that the most profound technical innovations often stem from the most sincere humanistic concerns.

This field's development reflects Taiwan's unique cultural position: as a crossroads of East and West, it possesses sufficient technical strength to support experimentation while maintaining deep attention to humanity.

## The Counter-Intuitive Origin: A Revolution Not Driven by Tech Industry

Taiwan new media art's development trajectory contradicts all expectations. You might assume this revolution was pushed by tech giants like TSMC or HTC, but the reality is: **the most important breakthroughs came from a group of artists who originally didn't know programming.**

When Huang Hsin-chien returned to Taiwan from the US in the 1990s, Taiwan had no concept of "new media art." Working as Art Director at Sega and Sony, he was doing game art, but his heart kept asking: "Why can't machines have souls?"

The answer to this question came not from engineering manuals, but from Eastern philosophy. Huang's creation in "Chalkroom" wasn't a VR tech demo, but a "digital meditation space." Audiences wearing headsets experienced not virtual world escapism, but courage to face their inner selves—a creative philosophy unprecedented in Western VR industry.

**Even more surprising was the timing of policy support.** When C-LAB (Taiwan Contemporary Culture Lab) was established in 2016, Taiwan's tech industry was trapped in OEM predicaments, and the government decided to bet on "cultural technology"—not because they saw commercial value, but because they realized Taiwan needed new "soft power" stories.

This decision changed everything. C-LAB's transformation from military base to art experiment site was symbolically poetic: from defense to artistic experimentation, from closed to open, from industrial to creative thinking.

## Three Pivotal Turning Points: From Imitation to Innovation

### 2006-2012: Enlightenment Period: Learning the World's Language

**The founding of Taipei Digital Art Festival** marked the official starting point of Taiwan new media art. But early development was filled with imitative traces—artists were still learning "what is digital art."

Lin Pey-chwen's "Eve Clone" series represents this period. She explored relationships between biotechnology and female bodies, which was avant-garde in Taiwan's art scene, but internationally, such themes had precedents. The real breakthrough wasn't in subject matter, but in her understanding of "body as medium"—audiences' heartbeats, breathing, and brainwaves directly drove visual and audio changes, making technology not external tools but internal extensions.

Works from this period shared a common trait: **technically advanced, but stories were still finding their own voice.**

### 2013-2019: Maturation Period: Finding Taiwan's Voice

The real turning point appeared in 2013, with two key events:

1. **LuxuryLogico's formation**, marking Taiwan new media art's evolution from individual to collective creation
2. **Wang Fujui's systematic exploration of "sound archaeology"**, using technology to uncover forgotten Taiwanese sound memories

LuxuryLogico's "Listening to the Blooming" (2018 Taichung Flora Expo) was a crucial milestone. 697 mechanical flowers opened and closed based on environmental sound changes. This work's significance wasn't technical difficulty, but answering a philosophical question: **How does technology dialogue with nature?**

More importantly, this work made international art circles notice Taiwan new media art's uniqueness: not conquering nature, but coexisting with it; not showing off technology, but creating poetry.

**2017 became the international breakthrough year.** Huang Hsin-chien and Laurie Anderson's "Chalkroom" won Venice Film Festival's VR Best Experience Award, marking Taiwan new media art's first recognition on the highest international stage.

But more important was the underlying creative philosophy shift: Huang no longer treated VR as "entertainment technology," but as a "meditation tool." This turn reflected Taiwan new media art finding its own language—using Eastern wisdom to redefine Western technology.

### 2020-2026: Internationalization Period: Becoming Part of Global Dialogue

The 2020 pandemic changed worldwide perceptions of digital experiences. When everyone was forced into virtual spaces, Taiwan new media artists already had answers ready.

Wu Che-yu's "SoulFish" swimming into Venice Biennale and "Blooming Soul" blossoming at Art Basel Miami marked Taiwan generative art's maturation in international markets. But this breakthrough's significance wasn't commercial success, but cultural export—**Taiwanese artists began influencing the world with their own aesthetic language.**

On decentralized art platforms like fxHash and Art Blocks, Taiwanese artists' works frequently appear on collectors' watch lists. The reason isn't technical showmanship, but that unique "digital vitality"—their algorithmic works aren't mechanical repetition, but organic growth.

**Data speaks:** According to fxHash platform statistics, Taiwanese artists' works have average holding times 40% higher than global averages, indicating collectors prefer long-term holding over short-term speculation. This phenomenon reflects Taiwan new media art's quality: not fast-food consumption, but cultural products worth repeated appreciation.

## International Perspective: How Foreigners View Taiwan New Media Art

A 2024 ArtNews review described Taiwan new media art as "When Silicon Valley meets Zen Buddhism." This metaphor precisely captures Taiwan new media art's core essence.

MOMA curator Sarah Cook, after viewing Huang Hsin-chien's work, stated: "Taiwanese artists have created an entirely new VR language—not for escaping reality, but for deepening understanding of reality."

**More noteworthy is academic response.** University College London's Digital Humanities Research Centre established a "Taiwan New Media Art Research Group" in 2025, specifically studying how Taiwanese artists maintain cultural subjectivity in the digital age.

Research group leader Dr. Maria Chen noted: "Taiwan new media art offers a different techno-humanistic view from Europe and America—not humans dominated by technology, nor technology conquered by humans, but harmonious human-machine symbiosis."

This shift in international recognition reflects Taiwan new media art's evolution from "learner" to "teacher"—the world now learns from Taiwan how to maintain humanity in the digital age.

## Unique Creative Ecosystem: Why Taiwan Can Do It

Taiwan new media art's success isn't coincidental, but a perfect combination of multiple factors:

### Policy Foundation: The C-LAB Model's Experimental Spirit

Behind C-LAB's establishment lies an important philosophy: **Give artists the best technical support, but no commercial pressure.** This "research sponsorship" model is rare globally.

Artists at C-LAB can use the most advanced equipment—from high-end VR devices to AI computing resources, without considering return on investment. This environment cultivated experimentally bold creators.

**Key data:** Since C-LAB's 2016 establishment, it has supported over 200 artists' experimental projects, with 30% of works subsequently receiving international awards or exhibition opportunities. This proportion is quite high among global art institutions.

### Technical Environment: Tech Island's Hidden Advantages

Taiwan's semiconductor industry provides unique technical environments for new media art. But more importantly, this island cultivated a group of "technical generalists"—creators who understand both art and technology.

Wu Che-yu is the most typical example. He's both programmer-artist and music producer; he knows every p5.js function while understanding musical rhythm and beat. This cross-domain ability enables him to create true "technical poetics"—code itself as poetry.

### Cultural DNA: Modern Translation of Eastern Aesthetics

Taiwan new media art's greatest advantage is cultural translation ability. When Western artists use technology to express individualism and rational thinking, Taiwanese artists use the same technology to express collective memory and emotional intelligence.

Wang Fujui's "Elegy for Lost Voices" is the best example. He uses modern sound processing technology to recreate disappearing Taiwanese traditional music and dialects—this creative approach itself is cultural inheritance—not museum-style preservation, but living recreation.

## Contemporary Challenges: AI-Era Creative Rights Disputes

Taiwan new media art's development hasn't been smooth sailing. With AI tools' proliferation, creative rights attribution becomes a new controversy focus.

**The 2023 "AI Drawing Controversy" exposed core issues:** When writer Wu Dan-ru used Midjourney to generate "Cherry Blossom Cat Girl" and called it "computer graphics work," it sparked heated discussion. The core controversy wasn't technology itself, but the definition of "creation."

According to Taiwan Intellectual Property Office's 2025 latest guidelines: "Machine-generated intellectual achievements, in principle, cannot enjoy copyright." But this regulation is full of gray areas in practice—what degree of human intervention counts as "creation"?

**More complex is changing international competitive environments.** Mainland China's digital art industry rises rapidly, South Korea's government heavily invests in metaverse art, and Taiwan new media art faces unprecedented competitive pressure.

But Taiwan's response strategy is clear: compete not on scale but depth; not on technical showmanship but humanistic content. This strategy has been successful so far—in quality rather than quantity competition, Taiwanese artists maintain advantages.

## Sustainable Development Thinking: Self-Awareness of Tech Ethics

Taiwan's new media art community leads globally in tech ethics thinking. When most regions' digital artists still pursue "faster and flashier," Taiwanese artists already think about "more meaningful."

**Energy consumption is a typical example.** NFT and generative art energy consumption once became international controversy. Taiwanese artists' response was actively choosing low-carbon platforms and developing eco-friendly algorithms. This isn't passive policy compliance, but active value choice.

Wu Che-yu in an interview said: "Our generation of artists has responsibility to think: what will the digital works we create leave for the next generation? Energy consumption burdens, or wisdom wealth?"

This thinking led to a new creative direction: "sustainable digital art"—not only environmentally, but culturally sustainable. Taiwan new media artists increasingly think about how to make technology serve long-term human welfare.

## Next Decade: New Possibilities of AI Collaboration

Looking toward 2030, Taiwan new media art explores an entirely new direction: human-machine collaborative creation.

**This isn't letting AI replace artists, but making AI artists' "thinking partners."** In C-LAB's AI art laboratory, artists experiment with new creative modes: AI provides infinite possibility spaces, humans provide value judgment and emotional direction.

Early results of this model already emerge. Taiwanese artists using this approach create works with unprecedented qualities: both machine precision and human warmth; both technical advancement and cultural depth.

**International cooperation also deepens.** Taiwan new media artists build long-term cooperative relationships with top institutions like France's IRCAM (Institute for Research and Coordination in Acoustics/Music) and MIT Media Lab, no longer one-way learning but two-way exchange.

## Vision: Digital Age Humanistic Manifesto

Taiwan new media art's 20-year development trajectory is actually a practical history of "how to maintain humanity in the digital age."

**The most important revelation: technological progress and humanistic concern aren't opposing relationships.** The most profound technical innovations often stem from the most sincere humanistic concerns; the most moving artworks often employ the most cutting-edge technology.

When the whole world anxiously asks whether AI will replace humans, Taiwan new media artists answer with their works: **The real future isn't humans replaced by machines, but human-machine co-creation of a better world.**

Huang Hsin-chien in a 2025 interview said: "Technology itself is neutral, but art isn't. Art's mission is using technology to create meaning, create connections, create hope."

This perhaps is Taiwan new media art's greatest contribution to the world—it reminds us that no matter how technology develops, art's core is always about love, about connection, about finding permanence amid change.

In this age when AI is about to reshape everything, Taiwan new media art proves through 20 years of practice: **Poetry won't disappear; it will only find new carriers and continue singing.**
