> **30-Second Overview:** Chiao An-pu, performing as "Deserts Chang" (張懸), became the defining voice of Taiwan's indie music scene. She wrote her signature song "Bao Bei" at age 13. In 2013, she held up a Republic of China flag during a concert in Manchester, UK — and China banned her. In 2015, she retired her stage name and vanished for three years. She returned as "Anpu," won Song of the Year at the Golden Melody Awards, then shocked Taiwan in 2024 by congratulating China on its national day. The same person lost her audience twice, for completely opposite reasons.

## The Flag in Manchester

November 2, 2013. Manchester, UK. A small concert venue, roughly five hundred people, mostly Chinese international students[^1]. A few Taiwanese students in the front row brought a Republic of China flag. Deserts Chang took it, held it against her chest, and said in English:

"It's just a flag. It shows where I come from. Why do you have to make it about politics?"[^2]

A Chinese student shouted from the audience: "No politics today!"

That one sentence ended Deserts Chang's career in China. Within days, a boycott campaign on Weibo forced the cancellation of her scheduled Beijing concert[^3]. Back in Taiwan, tens of thousands of netizens crowned her a "patriotic singer." She was stunned: "You've got me all wrong."[^4]

She had just held up a flag. But across the Taiwan Strait, nothing is ever "just" anything.

## A Rebellious Daughter

Chiao An-pu was born on May 30, 1981, in Taipei. Her father, Chiao Jen-ho, served as secretary-general of the Straits Exchange Foundation — Taiwan's semi-official body for negotiating with China — and was President Lee Teng-hui's chief speechwriter. Her grandfather was one of China's first-generation lawyers[^5]. Her brother, Chiao Yuan-pu, became Taiwan's most prominent classical music critic, interviewing fifty-five of the world's greatest pianists. Her sister went into law[^6]. Of the three children, she was the only one who refused the path their father expected.

At thirteen, she stormed out of the house after a family argument, humming a melody as she walked. That melody became "Bao Bei" (Baby)[^7] — "My baby, baby, let me give you something sweet, so you can sleep well tonight." It would become the lullaby every Taiwanese parent knows. But originally, it was a girl singing to comfort herself.

In high school, she started writing poetry and submitted work under the pen name "Chang Hsuan" (張懸). The character "hsuan" means suspended, hanging in limbo[^8]. She never finished high school.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> The daughter of a cross-strait negotiator chose a stage name that means "suspended" — as in unresolved. Probably not a coincidence.

## The Girl in the Live Houses

In 2003, Chiao An-pu took the stage at the Hohaiyan Rock Festival as vocalist of the band Mango Runs, winning both the Most Popular Act and Indie Music awards[^9]. Over the next few years, she became the most-played singer-songwriter in Taipei's live house circuit. By nineteen, she had written over a hundred songs.

Her debut album _My Life Will…_ came out in 2006 on Sony BMG, earning four nominations at the 18th Golden Melody Awards, including Best Mandarin Album[^10]. Some fans worried that signing with a major label would change her. She said it would not.

It did not. Four albums between 2006 and 2012, each less commercial than the last. The opening track of her 2012 album _Games We Play_ was called "Rose-Colored You," written for the activists on the streets[^11]. The following year, it won Best Lyricist at the 24th Golden Melody Awards.

A songwriter who wrote songs for protesters had reached the peak under the name Deserts Chang. Then she decided to kill that name.

## Sunflowers and the Last Tide

After the flag incident, Deserts Chang did not retreat. When Taiwan's Sunflower Movement erupted in March 2014, she posted repeatedly on Facebook, criticizing the government's forced passage of a trade agreement with China and urging people "not to give up your right to engage in civic issues"[^12]. She also spoke out for the anti-nuclear movement, water rights disputes, and marriage equality[^8]. "Rose-Colored You" became the unofficial soundtrack of street protests.

But she never wanted to be called a "political singer."

> **✦** "If Taiwan's national identity aspires to a higher international profile, it will inevitably be challenged. I'm not the first person to face this problem. If I didn't do it, someone else would." — Deserts Chang, December 2013[^2]

July 2015, Kaohsiung Arena. The final show of "To Ebb" (潮水箴言), a concert series she had been building since 2010, growing from thousand-seat clubs to ten-thousand-seat exhibition halls[^13]. After the final note, she announced: the name Deserts Chang ends here.

Her reason for reverting to her birth name was a single sentence: "Anpu is the name my parents gave me. I simply want them to see these two characters when they clip newspaper articles about me."[^14]

Then she disappeared.

## Three Years of Silence

During her three years away, she read, practiced calligraphy, wrote poetry, strengthened her instrumental skills, and studied stage production[^15]. She showed up occasionally at civic events, but did not sing, did not give interviews, did not explain.

The silence mattered. Over twelve years, Deserts Chang had calcified into a symbol: indie music, social activism, national identity. The symbol was too heavy. She needed to put it down to find out what was left underneath.

## The Person Who Came Back Is Called Anpu

In 2018, she returned as "Anpu" with a concert called "Refine" (煉雲) at Taipei Arena, performing twenty-two cover songs — not her own[^16]. It was not a comeback concert. It was a declaration: I am back, but I am not the person you remember.

In October 2022, she released _9522_ — sixteen tracks, almost all written between the ages of fourteen and seventeen. The cryptic album title hinted at the span from 1995 to 2022, twenty-seven years[^17]. A forty-year-old woman singing songs a teenager had written. She said: "It's not just the forty-year-old me going back to sing things from twenty-seven years ago."[^18]

In 2023, "A Flash and How It Lasts" (最好的時光) won Song of the Year at the 34th Golden Melody Awards[^19]. On that night, the name on the trophy was not Deserts Chang. It was Anpu. Her first Golden Melody Award under her real name.

> **💡 Did you know?**
> When Chiao Yuan-pu interviews world-class pianists, he brings Deserts Chang's CDs as gifts. He introduces her as "the musician in our family."[^6]

## October First

1. **2013/11** — Holds flag in Manchester; China bans her
2. **2014/3** — Speaks out during the Sunflower Movement
3. **2015/7** — Final "To Ebb" concert; retires the name Deserts Chang
4. **2018** — Returns as Anpu with "Refine"
5. **2022/10** — Releases _9522_
6. **2023** — Golden Melody Award, Song of the Year
7. **2024/10/1** — Congratulates China on its national day

On October 1, 2024, Anpu's management company posted a handwritten note on Weibo: "Wishing the 75th anniversary of the founding of New China — may the people be harmonious, and peace extend ten thousand miles." Signed: Anpu[^20].

The same person who was banned from China for holding a Republic of China flag eleven years earlier was now wishing the People's Republic of China a happy birthday.

Taiwanese fans were not angry. They were heartbroken. The person who wrote "Rose-Colored You," who spoke up during the Sunflower Movement, who said "It's just a flag" in Manchester — how could she write that letter? Her close friend He Hsin-sui tried to defend her and was flooded with criticism[^21]. A columnist called it "a long-planned pledge of allegiance"[^22].

Eleven days later, October 12, at the Takao Rock Festival in Kaohsiung. Before Anpu took the stage, the crowd was already waving Taiwanese independence flags. Someone shouted: "Chiao An-pu — why?"

She did not answer directly. She said something else.

## You Can Be Yourself

"At my shows, you are always free and safe. The world is big, and I can't guarantee that every place is the same. But at least where I perform, you can be yourself."[^23]

Eleven years ago in Manchester, she said, "It's just a flag." Eleven years later at the Takao Rock Festival, she said, "You can be yourself." Both sentences point to the same thing: a refusal to define your freedom for you, and a refusal to let you define hers.

You can say she changed. You can also say she has been doing the same thing all along — living in the gray zone where no flag is big enough and no name can contain who she is.

The answer depends on what you think that flag represented. And that, from 2013 to now, is the question she has been asking.
