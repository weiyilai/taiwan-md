> **30-second overview:** In 1971, a war orphan started a business in Xinzhuang with NT$300,000.
> 55 years later, he built the world's largest switching power supply company with revenue exceeding NT$555 billion.
> Most remarkably: he got his name into space—asteroid 168126 is called "Chengbruce."

April 4, 1971, Min'an Road, Xinzhuang Township, Taipei County. 36-year-old Bruce Cheng and two partners began assembling TV transformers in a two-story building with NT$300,000 in savings. This war orphan, who fled to Taiwan at age 13 during the Chinese Civil War, had no idea he was launching a commercial empire that would reshape global thinking about electricity.

55 years later, when you charge your phone, the technology behind it likely comes from Delta Electronics. When Google's AI processes your search queries, the data center's power systems are probably Delta-manufactured. From personal computers to supercomputing data centers, from Tesla charging stations to wind power converters—Delta Electronics has quietly become the "invisible infrastructure" of modern digital life.

**But the most stunning part of this story isn't the revenue figures—it's how one man turned "environmental protection" from a moral slogan into competitive advantage.**

## A Taiwanese Entrepreneur in Space

On April 1, 2006, Taiwan's Central University Lulin Observatory discovered an asteroid numbered 168126. After certification by the International Astronomical Union, this asteroid was officially named "Chengbruce" in 2008—it will orbit forever in space, carrying the name of a Taiwanese entrepreneur.

This wasn't vanity purchased with money. Bruce Cheng funded astronomical research for over a decade, donating for Lulin Observatory's 2-meter telescope, rebuilding Central University's Science Building I observatory, and establishing the "Delta Electronics Young Astronomer Lecture Series." In 2009, during the UN's International Year of Astronomy, he served as Taiwan's "Star Ambassador."

Why would a power supply manufacturer invest in basic science? Cheng's answer was pragmatic: "If humans can't properly care for the Earth we inhabit, dreaming about space exploration is just wishful thinking."

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Asteroid "Chengbruce" has an orbital period of about 6.1 years. This is the first asteroid named after a Taiwanese entrepreneur.
> This long-term thinking explains why Delta has maintained technological leadership for half a century.

## From TV Components to AI Era Power Management

The story returns to that Xinzhuang factory. When Delta Electronics was founded in 1971, it primarily produced TV coils and IF transformers—components for black-and-white television sets. Bruce Cheng learned theory at National Cheng Kung University's electrical engineering department, craftsmanship repairing aircraft instruments at Asia Airlines, and management at TRW.

But the decision that truly changed Delta's destiny came in 1975: **switching to switching power supply technology.**

This seemed like a technical detail but was actually a commercial revolution. Traditional linear power supplies were bulky and inefficient, typically achieving only 50-60% conversion efficiency. Switching power supplies were compact and efficient, achieving 80-90% conversion efficiency. When personal computers emerged in the 1980s, Delta was perfectly positioned, becoming a power supplier for brands like Apple and Compaq.

**In 2002, Delta surpassed America's Astec to become the world's largest power supply manufacturer.**

But the real strategic turning point came in 2000. As the dot-com bubble burst and tech companies contracted, Cheng proposed what seemed "too advanced" at the time: the corporate mission "To provide innovative, clean and efficient energy solutions for a better tomorrow." In an era before ESG concepts became popular, many viewed this decision as impractical.

Looking back 20 years later, this was Delta's wisest strategy.

## The Invisible but Omnipresent Power Empire

What scale of commercial empire is today's Delta Electronics?

According to the latest 2025 financial reports, Delta achieved annual revenue of NT$554.9 billion (approximately US$17.5 billion), with about 88,000 employees globally and over 200 operational sites across 38 countries. In the global switching power supply market, Delta holds approximately 20-25% market share, ranking solidly first worldwide.

**"One in four computers globally uses Delta power technology."**

Even more striking is the change brought by the AI wave: AI-related business accounted for 23% of Delta's total revenue in 2024, holding about 50% market share in AI server power supplies. Google, Microsoft, and Amazon's data centers are all its customers.

## New Opportunities and Challenges in the AI Wave

As artificial intelligence applications explode, global data center power demand is surging. Tech giants like Meta and Google are building ultra-large data centers consuming 1GW (gigawatt)—equivalent to a city of 260,000 people.

**The challenge lies in the speed of technological evolution.** New-generation AI chips demand unprecedented power management requirements, with power consumption jumping from traditional 400-800 watts to 8,000 watts or higher. Delta is developing 800-volt high-voltage DC power systems and ±400-volt DC systems, scheduled for mass production in the second half of 2026.

According to Taipei Times reports, Delta's facilities in Taiwan, Thailand, and the US are operating at full capacity. After adding three plants in Thailand in 2025, capacity expansion will continue this year.

**But Delta's competitive advantage isn't just in capacity—it's in system integration capability.** It's not merely a component supplier but a system vendor providing complete solutions. When automakers need electric vehicle powertrains, Delta can provide everything from onboard chargers to drive motors. When data centers need energy-saving retrofits, Delta can provide one-stop service from power supplies to air conditioning.

## The Business Philosophy of Energy Efficiency: Turning Environmental Protection into Competitive Advantage

Delta's most stunning numbers aren't revenue figures but its contribution to global energy conservation. According to official statistics, from 2010 to 2023, Delta's high-efficiency products cumulatively saved over 40 billion kWh for global customers, equivalent to reducing 21 million tons of carbon emissions—exceeding many countries' annual total emissions.

**The key is that Delta transformed sustainability from slogan into business model.**

In 2021, Delta became Taiwan's first manufacturing company to join the RE100 initiative, committing to 100% renewable energy across global operations by 2030. In 2022, Delta began implementing internal carbon pricing—charging $100-300 per ton of carbon emissions. Carbon emissions decreased 13.5% the following year, dropping further by 39% in 2023.

> **📊 Data Source**
> According to CSR@CommonWealth reports, internal carbon pricing revenues fund Delta's energy-saving investments.
> One production unit was charged $24 million in carbon fees in 2022. After investing $22 million in energy efficiency,
> carbon emissions dropped 17% the following year, with carbon fees decreasing 38%.

Cheng often used one concept to encourage employees: "Just improving server power supply efficiency by 1% can save 1MW of system power consumption!" This isn't moral persuasion but mathematical fact—in an era of rising global energy costs, energy efficiency equals competitive advantage.

## Brand Breakthrough for the Contract Manufacturing Giant

Delta Electronics' greatest challenge is balancing contract manufacturing thinking with brand innovation. From 1980-2000, it was primarily an ODM supplier for multinational companies, bringing stable orders but limiting control over technological development.

The turning point came in the 21st century. Delta began heavily investing in its own brand "DELTA" while maintaining contract manufacturing business. This dual-track strategy maintained cash flow while building brand value.

Results were significant: Delta holds about 20% market share in European electric vehicle charging and ranks among the top three globally in UPS systems. These achievements prove Taiwanese companies can build brand advantages in technology-intensive fields, not just contract manufacturing.

## Inspiration from a Drop of Polluted Water

Bruce Cheng's environmental beliefs originated from an experience while working at TRW before founding Delta. When the factory discharged wastewater, he saw fish floating belly-up. At that moment, he decided: "I will absolutely never engage in environmentally polluting business."

Fifty years later, this decision became Delta's core competitive advantage. As the world pursues net-zero emissions, Delta's energy-saving technology isn't just products—it's humanity's tools for addressing climate change.

**Environmental protection isn't a cost—it's the source of competitive advantage.** This statement sounded idealistic in 2000 but has become business common sense in 2026.

## The Wisdom of Succession

In 2012, 76-year-old Bruce Cheng announced retirement, passing management to his eldest son Ping Cheng. This was a rare case of smooth succession in Taiwan's business circles. Ping Cheng worked at Delta for over 20 years, starting as a grassroots engineer and developing deep understanding of company culture and technology.

Under Ping Cheng's leadership, Delta continues deepening the "To provide innovative, clean and efficient energy solutions for a better tomorrow" mission while strengthening digital transformation and AI applications. In December 2024, Delta launched the "Delta Net Zero Science Laboratory" in Tainan Science Park—Taiwan's first megawatt-level water electrolysis hydrogen production and fuel cell testing platform.

This shows Delta isn't just executing existing strategies but preparing for the next generation's energy revolution.

## Epitome of an Era

Delta Electronics' story perfectly epitomizes Taiwan's manufacturing industry transformation and upgrading. It demonstrates how Taiwanese companies evolved from contract manufacturing thinking to technological innovation, from cost competition to value creation, from local operations to global presence.

More importantly, Delta proved that "manufacturing" and "sustainable development" aren't opposed but can mutually reinforce each other. In an era of global net-zero pursuits, Delta's green technology—developed 20 years ago—has become its greatest moat.

**A 13-year-old war orphan spent half a century building a power empire and getting his name into space.**

This story tells us Taiwan isn't just "Manufacturing Island" but "Innovation Island." In the next generation's technological revolution, we have reason to expect more such Taiwanese legends.

As AI reshapes the world and climate change redefines business rules, Delta Electronics is ready. It has spent 50 years proving one thing: true competitive advantage isn't about doing things cheaper, but doing them smarter.

> **✦** "Energy doesn't disappear, it transforms."
> —Bruce Cheng

In a world needing more efficiency, more greenness, and more intelligence, Delta Electronics isn't just Taiwan's corporate exemplar—it's humanity's technological progress catalyst.
